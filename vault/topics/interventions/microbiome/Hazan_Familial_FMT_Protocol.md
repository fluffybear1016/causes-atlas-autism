# Hazan Familial FMT Protocol — Deep Dive

This is a structured synthesis of what Sabine Hazan's familial-FMT-for-autism protocol actually consists of, pieced together from her own X feed, her ACG 2025 presentation, her published 19-year-old case (ResearchGate), and her public conference talks. **None of this is a substitute for direct consultation with Dr. Hazan or another qualified physician.** This is for understanding the mechanism and structure of the intervention, not as a self-administration guide. FMT outside of an FDA IND or clinical trial is not safe and not appropriate for autism in any DIY form.

For background on why this intervention is being used at all, see [[Hazan_Sabine]].

## What the intervention actually is

**Sibling-donor fecal microbiota transplantation, performed under FDA Investigational New Drug authorization (ClinicalTrials.gov NCT04878718), with full pre-and-post 16S rRNA sequencing at ProgenaBiome.** That's the core protocol in one sentence.

What makes it different from the random-donor or commercial-product FMT that has been studied in C. difficile colitis (Rebyota, Vowst):

- **Donor selection is precision-matched** to the specific recipient's microbial gaps, not a generic "healthy donor" pool. Hazan's lab data shows that siblings share most of their microbial ecology because they share environment, diet, and (often) maternal seeding history, which substantially raises the probability that transplanted microbes "take" rather than getting cleared by the recipient's immune system.
- **The donor is sequenced first** to confirm the donor's microbiome actually contains the species the recipient is missing — particularly *Bifidobacterium*. If the sibling is also Bifidobacterium-depleted, they're not a useful donor for that target organism.
- **It's iterative.** Multiple rounds, monitored by sequencing, with intervention adjusted based on what's actually re-emerging in the recipient's gut.

## Step-by-step (synthesized from public statements)

### 1. Comprehensive pre-treatment workup

- **Full metagenomic sequencing** of the recipient's stool at ProgenaBiome. 16S rRNA at minimum; deeper shotgun sequencing in research-arm cases. Goal: identify exactly which organisms are missing or overrepresented.
- **Genetic sequencing** of the child where indicated — Hazan is explicit that some autism is genetic, some is microbiome-driven, some is both. The genetic workup helps stratify.
- **Standard medical workup** — GI symptoms, dietary history, prior antibiotic exposure, vaccination history (relevant because some exposures are correlated with microbiome collapse in her data), perinatal history (C-section vs vaginal birth substantially affects baseline microbiome).
- **Behavioral baseline** — speech, aggression, sensory profile, GI complaints, sleep — for outcome tracking.

### 2. Sibling-donor identification and screening

- Healthy neurotypical sibling preferred (closest microbial overlap with the recipient).
- Donor stool sequenced to confirm it contains the missing organisms (especially Bifidobacterium species).
- Standard donor-screening for transmissible pathogens per FMT clinical-trial protocols (CMV, EBV, hepatitis, HIV, parasites, multidrug-resistant organisms — these are non-negotiable safety steps in any FMT, IND or otherwise).

### 3. Familial FMT under NCT04878718

- The FMT itself is administered per Hazan's IND protocol — she has not publicly released the exact route, dosing, or processing steps because of her stated concern about retraction targeting.
- What is publicly known: the protocol is sibling-derived, processed by her lab, and administered with full FDA oversight (NCT04878718 is a real, documented investigational new drug authorization).

### 4. Iterative reflorization with Bifidobacterium focus

- The intervention is not one-shot. The twin case ran over a 9-month protocol. Multiple administrations, with intervening sequencing, until the missing organisms (especially Bifidobacterium) durably re-establish.
- This is the "reflorization" concept Hazan repeatedly references — restoring the missing flora rather than just dumping in a generic mix.

### 5. Adjunctive and supportive measures

Context-dependent, not uniform across patients:

- **Diet** — Bifidobacterium-supportive nutrition (the documentation of nutritional healing in autism that Documenting Hope has produced is what Hazan publicly endorsed). Specific foods that support Bifidobacterium include human milk oligosaccharides where appropriate, certain prebiotic fibers (galactooligosaccharides, FOS, inulin), and avoidance of agents that suppress Bifidobacterium.
- **Ivermectin (case-by-case, not default).** Her lab has shown IVM increases Bifidobacterium within 24 hours in *some* recipients. She has explicitly warned: *do not* give IVM to a young child whose Bifidobacterium is still intact, because it can deplete it. This is a sequencing-guided decision, not a blanket protocol.
- **L. reuteri (selective, not default).** Most kids cannot tolerate it because it was never part of their native microbiome. Used only when sequencing supports it.
- **Avoidance of microbiome-disruptive exposures** — antibiotics where avoidable, glyphosate (see [[Seneff_Stephanie]]), some pharmaceuticals known to suppress Bifidobacterium, mRNA shots (Hazan has presented data on Bifidobacterium loss post-mRNA in non-autism contexts; she treats vaccine exposure as one of several environmental hits worth considering in the broader microbiome story).

### 6. Post-intervention sequencing and long-term monitoring

- Post-treatment sequencing at multiple timepoints.
- Free testing offered through ProgenaBiome research arm to grow the long-term n-of-1 dataset: *"Don't forget to give me your sample when back and better."*
- Behavioral / speech / GI outcomes correlated with sequencing trajectory.

## The specific outcome biomarker

**Speech return correlates temporally with Bifidobacterium re-emergence on sequencing.** This is the most striking observation across her case series. The twins' speech recovery was specifically anchored to the post-treatment timepoint when Bifidobacterium populations re-established on sequencing. The 19-year-old case showed similar correlation. This is the kind of mechanistic-biomarker-coupled-to-clinical-outcome data that the atlas explicitly weights highly per [[CLAUDE]] §7.

It is also consistent with what is known about Bifidobacterium's role:
- SCFA (short-chain fatty acid) production — propionate, butyrate, acetate — directly affecting blood-brain-barrier integrity, microglial activation, and neuronal energetics.
- Vagal afferent stimulation — gut-brain communication.
- GABA precursor production — relevant to the GABA/glutamate excitatory-inhibitory balance hypothesis (see [[HYP-0071]] and [[MEC-0034]]).
- Tryptophan metabolism — serotonin precursor pathway.
- Immune tolerance — Treg induction, cytokine modulation, mast-cell stabilization (see [[Theoharides_Theoharis]]).

Each of these mechanisms is plausible. The convergence of "Bifidobacterium recovers, speech returns" across cases is what makes the protocol real rather than coincidence.

## Limits and caveats (Hazan's own framing)

- **Not all autistic children will respond.** Hazan is explicit. Microbiome intervention helps the microbiome-subgroup. Some kids have predominantly genetic causation, some have neurological causation, some are mixed. The pre-treatment sequencing is what decides whether this is the right intervention.
- **Severity matters but doesn't predict in the obvious direction.** Some severely affected nonverbal kids respond dramatically (twins, 19yo). Mild cases sometimes have less Bifidobacterium loss to begin with.
- **Time-course is months, not weeks.** Twin case was 9 months. Parents expecting overnight changes will be disappointed.
- **Donor matters.** A sibling who is also Bifidobacterium-depleted is not a useful donor. Sequencing is non-negotiable.
- **Maintenance matters.** Re-exposure to whatever caused the Bifidobacterium loss in the first place can undo the gains. This is why the post-treatment monitoring exists.

## Where to actually pursue this

**ProgenaBiome / Microbiome Research Foundation** is the place. Hazan's NCT04878718 is the current gold-standard pathway. Her practice is in Ventura County, California; she takes patients from elsewhere where logistics permit.

DIY FMT for autism is **not** safe. Donor screening is medically nontrivial, the route of administration matters, processing matters, and the regulatory pathway matters. The atlas does not endorse any DIY approach.

If geography or finances are the limit, [[Adams_James]]'s Microbiota Transfer Therapy (MTT) trials at Arizona State are the closest peer-reviewed analog and have been run as formal Phase 2 studies in autism with structured oral-preparation protocols.

## The science around it (for the people who want to read further)

- **Kang et al. 2017 (PMID 28122648)** — Original MTT autism trial (Adams group), 18 children, sustained behavioral/GI improvements at 8 weeks.
- **Kang et al. 2019 (PMID 30967657)** — 2-year follow-up of the MTT cohort, *Scientific Reports*. Improvements largely sustained. This is the closest peer-reviewed evidence for the durability hypothesis Hazan makes from her own series.
- **Hazan, Haroon, Jordan et al. 2024 (PMID 38715916)** — *"Improvements in Gut Microbiome Composition and Clinical Symptoms Following Familial Fecal Microbiota Transplantation in a Nineteen-Year-Old Adolescent With Severe Autism."* **Journal of Medical Cases**. Peer-reviewed publication of the 19-year-old case. First documented severe-ASD case using a family-member donor.
- **Hazan ACG 2025 abstract** — *A Potential Microbiome Signature Showcasing Improvement of Autism in Two Identical Twins.* Outstanding Presentation Award.
- **Sharon et al., Vuong et al., Hsiao et al.** — Mainstream basic-science gut-brain-axis literature on microbiome-behavior connections, mechanistic underpinnings.

## Bottom line for parents

If your child is severely affected, has documented GI symptoms, has a history of antibiotic exposure or other microbiome insults, and has not responded to behavioral interventions alone — getting full microbiome sequencing done is the first concrete step. ProgenaBiome offers this. If the sequencing shows Bifidobacterium depletion, Hazan's IND-protocol familial FMT is the most aggressive evidence-supported intervention currently available. James Adams's Arizona State MTT trials are the next-most-formal pathway. Both are real options. Neither is universally available, neither is guaranteed to work, and both require substantial commitment of time and (currently) personal funds.

This is not yet standard care. It should be. The work of getting it there is the work that Hazan, Adams, Frye, Rossignol, Naviaux, Theoharides, and the broader functional-medicine autism community is doing right now.
