# 🧪 Methylation Pathway Interventions for Autism — Overview

The methylation cycle (folate ↔ methionine ↔ transsulfuration) is dysregulated in a major subgroup of autistic children — possibly 30-50%. It's the biochemical machinery responsible for: DNA methylation (epigenetic gene regulation), neurotransmitter synthesis, glutathione production (the brain's main antioxidant), homocysteine handling, and cellular detoxification. When it's broken, multiple downstream systems suffer simultaneously.

The good news: **this layer is highly modifiable**, and interventions targeting it produced the atlas's highest-scoring intervention ([[INT-0001 Leucovorin (folinic acid)]] at csrs 83.35).

## When to consider this class

Strong indication:
- **FRAA-positive** (folate receptor alpha autoantibody) — leucovorin-responsive
- Language delay, especially regression of acquired language
- Family history of MTHFR variants
- Walsh undermethylator personality cluster (high inner tension, perfectionism, OCD features, seasonal allergies)
- Walsh overmethylator personality cluster (anxious, low motivation, paradoxical histamine elevation)
- Low SAM/SAH ratio on testing
- Elevated homocysteine
- Low glutathione (GSH/GSSG ratio)

Weaker indication:
- Pure microbiome-driven case without methylation involvement (microbiome class first)
- Pure mitochondrial primary case (mito class first)

The two classes do overlap heavily — many kids benefit from BOTH simultaneously.

## The intervention options

### 1. Leucovorin (folinic acid) — the calibration anchor

**The single highest-evidence intervention in pediatric biomedical autism medicine.** [[Frye_Richard]]'s body of work established that 70-75% of autistic children test positive for folate receptor alpha autoantibodies (FRAAs) which block folate transport across the blood-brain barrier. Leucovorin bypasses this blockade.

- **Form:** Calcium folinate (the reduced folate form), prescription
- **Dose:** Typically 0.5-2 mg/kg/day divided BID, titrated based on response
- **Evidence:** [[SRC-000012 27752075]] Frye 2018 RCT (Mol Psychiatry) — folinic acid improves verbal communication in language-impaired ASD; effect size larger in FRAA-positive subgroup
- **Atlas record:** [[INT-0001 Leucovorin (folinic acid)]] — csrs 83.35
- **Researcher:** [[Frye_Richard]]
- **Test before starting:** FRAA antibody panel (Religious Sisters of Mercy / Ramaekers lab; some commercial labs)

### 2. Methyl-B12 (subcutaneous and oral)

Bypasses methylation cycle defects when B12 metabolism is the bottleneck.

- **Form:** Methylcobalamin (active form), preferred over cyanocobalamin
- **Routes:** Subcutaneous injection (the [[Neubrander_James]] protocol — every 3 days), sublingual lozenge, oral
- **Dose:** Subcutaneous: ~64.5 mcg/kg every 3 days (Neubrander); oral: 1,000-5,000 mcg daily
- **Evidence:** [[SRC-000040 19056591]] James 2009 (Am J Clin Nutr) — methylcobalamin + folinic acid normalizes glutathione redox status
- **Researcher:** [[James_Jill]] (biochemistry foundation), [[Frye_Richard]] (clinical translation)

### 3. Methylated folate (5-MTHF)

Alternative to leucovorin for MTHFR-variant carriers who can't activate folic acid efficiently.

- **Form:** L-5-methyltetrahydrofolate (active form), preferred over folic acid (synthetic)
- **Dose:** 400-2000 mcg/day depending on needs
- **Evidence:** Particularly important for the ~50% of population with MTHFR C677T or A1298C variants
- **Atlas record:** [[INT-0008 Methylated folate (5-MTHF) — preconception/prenatal]]
- **Caution:** Overmethylators can feel worse on methyl folate; needs biotype calibration

### 4. SAM-e (S-adenosylmethionine)

Provides methyl groups directly, bypassing some methylation cycle defects.

- **Dose:** 200-800 mg/day (children typically 100-400 mg)
- **Use case:** Particularly useful for undermethylators with low SAM/SAH ratio
- **Caution:** Not for overmethylators; not for bipolar-spectrum (can trigger mania)

### 5. Betaine / TMG (trimethylglycine)

Alternative methyl donor; converts homocysteine to methionine via BHMT pathway.

- **Dose:** 500-3000 mg/day
- **Use case:** Especially when homocysteine is elevated
- **Caution:** Can elevate cholesterol in some

### 6. Glutathione precursors

Supports the antioxidant arm of the methylation cycle.

- **NAC (N-acetylcysteine)** — 600-2400 mg/day; foundational glutathione precursor
- **Glycine** — supports glutathione synthesis
- **Liposomal glutathione** — direct delivery (oral, nebulized, transdermal)
- **Whey protein (undenatured)** — provides cysteine + glutamic acid
- **Selenium** — cofactor for glutathione peroxidase
- **Alpha-lipoic acid** — recycles oxidized glutathione

### 7. Cofactors

The methylation cycle requires multiple cofactors:

- **Vitamin B6 / P5P (pyridoxal-5-phosphate)** — active form, often poorly converted from B6
- **Vitamin B2 / Riboflavin** — particularly riboflavin-5-phosphate
- **Magnesium** — Mg-glycinate, Mg-malate, Mg-threonate (BBB-crossing)
- **Zinc** — important for many methylation enzymes
- **Choline** — alternate methyl donor pathway
- **Molybdenum** — particularly important for CBS upregulators

## Walsh biotype framework

[[Walsh_William]]'s framework subdivides methylation issues into clinically meaningful subtypes that respond to different protocols:

**Undermethylators** (~30-40% of autism)
- Low whole-blood histamine
- Low SAM/SAH ratio
- Treatment: Methyl donors (methionine, SAM-e, methyl-B12, sometimes 5-MTHF)
- Caution: Folate can sometimes worsen; case-by-case

**Overmethylators** (~5-10% of autism)
- High whole-blood histamine
- High SAM/SAH ratio
- Treatment: Folate-cautious; niacinamide, B6, sometimes folate-blocking strategies
- Caution: Methyl donors can worsen

**Pyrolurics**
- Elevated urinary kryptopyrroles
- Treatment: B6 (P5P), zinc, GLA (evening primrose oil), magnesium

**Copper-overload phenotype**
- Elevated free copper, low zinc
- Treatment: Zinc supplementation, molybdenum, metallothionein-promoting protocols

## Yasko SNP-targeted framework

[[Yasko_Amy]]'s framework operates at SNP-level resolution: each child's specific variants in MTHFR, MTRR, MTR, COMT, CBS, BHMT, AHCY, MAT, SUOX, NOS determine which methyl donors will help vs hurt.

Workup: 23andMe + StrateGene / SelfDecode / Nutrigenomic Test Panel for SNP profile.

Yasko Protocol systematizes nutrient sequencing: clear toxicants first → support transsulfuration → support B12/B6 → gradually introduce methyl donors with monitoring.

## Workup before intervention

Order before starting:

**Foundational:**
- **FRAA antibody panel** — folate receptor alpha autoantibodies (most important single test)
- **Plasma SAM/SAH ratio** — methylation capacity
- **Plasma methionine, homocysteine, cysteine, taurine** — methionine cycle
- **Plasma + RBC folate** — folate sufficiency
- **B12 + methylmalonic acid (MMA)** — B12 sufficiency
- **Active B6 (P5P)** — B6 sufficiency
- **Whole-blood histamine** — methylation biotype proxy (Walsh)
- **Urinary kryptopyrroles** — pyroluria screen

**SNP profile:**
- **MTHFR genotype (C677T + A1298C)** — most important single SNPs
- **23andMe + interpretation** (StrateGene, SelfDecode, Nutrigenomic Test Panel) — comprehensive SNP profile

**Adjunctive:**
- **GSH/GSSG redox** — oxidative stress
- **Plasma + serum copper, plasma zinc, ceruloplasmin** — Cu:Zn balance
- **Mosaic OAT** — broad metabolomic context

## Practical workflow

1. **Get FRAA antibody panel + MTHFR genotype + plasma SAM/SAH** as starter workup.
2. **If FRAA-positive:** Start leucovorin trial under [[Frye_Richard]]-trained or MAPS clinician.
3. **If MTHFR-variant + low SAM/SAH:** Start methylated folate or methyl-B12, biotype-calibrated.
4. **Add cofactors** (B6/P5P, B2, magnesium, zinc) per status.
5. **Reassess at 8-12 weeks** — repeat biomarkers, track behavior.
6. **Iterate** based on response. Some kids need addition of NAC for glutathione, betaine for homocysteine, etc.
7. **Combine with diet + microbiome work** — methylation cycle interacts heavily with both.

## Bottom line

If your child shows any indication of methylation dysfunction (FRAA-positive, MTHFR variants, language delay, low SAM/SAH), this class is one of the highest-leverage you can engage. Effect sizes in stratified subgroups are large. The workup is accessible. The interventions are well-tolerated and have decades of clinical experience behind them.

Leucovorin specifically is the single intervention most likely to produce dramatic improvement in a child who is in the right subgroup. It should be on every workup short list.
