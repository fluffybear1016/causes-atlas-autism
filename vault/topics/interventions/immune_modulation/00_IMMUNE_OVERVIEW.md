# 🛡️ Immune Modulation Interventions for Autism — Overview

Neuroinflammation, microglial activation, mast cell activation, and post-infectious autoimmune syndromes (PANS/PANDAS) all converge in a substantial subgroup of autistic children. The immune system is dysregulated; modulating it produces measurable functional improvement. This class covers the spectrum from gentle (antihistamines, luteolin) to aggressive (IVIG, plasmapheresis).

## When to consider this class

Strong indication:
- **Sudden-onset OCD, tics, anxiety, eating restriction, sensory regression** — likely PANS/PANDAS subgroup
- Chronic allergies / atopic disease (eczema, asthma, food allergies)
- Recurring rashes, flushing, abdominal pain, GI dysmotility — likely MCAS
- Regression following infection, vaccination, or other immune challenge
- Family history of autoimmune disease
- Elevated cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-17)
- Elevated tryptase or methylhistamine
- Positive Cunningham Panel
- Positive ANA, RF, thyroid antibodies, gluten antibodies

Weaker indication:
- Pure non-inflammatory autism without immune markers
- Pure genetic syndromic without inflammation

## The intervention spectrum

### 1. First-line / gentle

**Antihistamines (H1 + H2 combination)**
- H1: cetirizine 5-10 mg/day, fexofenadine 30-180 mg/day, loratadine 5-10 mg/day, hydroxyzine 0.5-2 mg/kg/day (more sedating)
- H2: famotidine 0.5-1 mg/kg/day, ranitidine (where still available)
- Use: First-line for any inflammatory or allergic features; foundational for MCAS protocol
- Researcher: [[Theoharides_Theoharis]]

**Cromolyn sodium (oral)**
- Stabilizes mast cells in GI tract specifically
- Dose: 100-200 mg before meals, four times daily
- Use: GI-prominent MCAS, food sensitivity, intestinal mast cell disease
- Prescription-required

**Ketotifen**
- Combined H1 antihistamine + mast cell stabilizer
- Dose: 1-4 mg/day
- Use: When H1+H2+cromolyn isn't enough
- Available compounded in US

**Quercetin**
- Bioflavonoid mast cell stabilizer
- Dose: 250-500 mg twice daily (children); 500-1000 mg twice daily (older)
- OTC, well-tolerated

**Luteolin** — see deep-dive at [[topics/interventions/immune_modulation/Luteolin|Luteolin deep-dive]]
- Bioflavonoid; BBB-crossing (in lipid-formulated form); stabilizes mast cells AND microglia
- Best-evidence formulation: NeuroProtek or equivalent (luteolin + quercetin + rutin in olive pomace oil)
- Dose: ~100 mg luteolin per 10 kg/day, divided BID-TID (per Taliou 2013)
- Atlas record: [[INT-0106]]
- Researcher: [[Theoharides_Theoharis]]
- Pilot trial: Taliou 2013 ([PMID 23688534](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23688534/)); cytokine signature: Tsilioni 2015 ([PMID 26418275](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26418275/))
- Responder profile: elevated IL-6/TNF-α + atopic/GI features

### 2. Second-line / immunomodulators

**Low-dose naltrexone (LDN)**
- Sub-clinical opioid receptor modulation; produces paradoxical anti-inflammatory effect
- Dose: 0.5-4.5 mg at bedtime (adults); pediatric dosing typically 0.1-0.5 mg/kg
- Mechanism: TLR4 modulation, microglial regulation, increased endorphins
- Use: Chronic neuroinflammation, autoimmune-spectrum, PANS-spectrum, IBD comorbidity
- Atlas: [[INT-0006 Low-dose naltrexone (LDN)]]
- Compounded prescription

**Antibiotic prophylaxis (for PANDAS)**
- Penicillin (250-500 mg twice daily) or azithromycin (500 mg every other day) prophylactically
- Use: PANDAS confirmed by Cunningham Panel + history of strep flares
- Researcher: [[Swedo_Sue]], [[Cunningham_Madeleine]]

**NSAIDs (for PANS/PANDAS)**
- Ibuprofen, naproxen — anti-inflammatory; first-line PANS treatment
- Course: Higher dose for 4-6 weeks during acute flare, then taper

### 3. Third-line / aggressive

**IVIG (intravenous immunoglobulin)**
- 1-2 g/kg infusion; usually monthly courses
- Use: Severe PANS/PANDAS, refractory autoimmune presentations
- Cost: Significant ($5,000-$15,000 per course)
- Specialist administration required

**Plasmapheresis / plasma exchange**
- Removes circulating autoantibodies
- Use: Severe PANS/PANDAS not responsive to IVIG
- Hospital-based; specialist administration

**Rituximab (anti-CD20 B-cell depletion)**
- Severe autoimmune cases; rare in autism context
- Hematology / rheumatology supervision

### 4. Microglial-specific

**Minocycline**
- Tetracycline antibiotic with documented microglial-modulating activity
- Dose: 50-100 mg twice daily
- Use: Chronic neuroinflammation
- Caution: Long-term use has antibiotic risks; permanent skin discoloration possible
- Weekly off-label cycling sometimes used

**Pioglitazone**
- PPAR-γ agonist (diabetes drug); anti-inflammatory effects
- Dose: 15-30 mg/day
- Use: Chronic neuroinflammation
- Caution: Weight gain, fluid retention

### 5. Cell therapy as immune modulation

See [[topics/interventions/cell_therapy/00_CELL_THERAPY_OVERVIEW]]

Mesenchymal stem cells produce paracrine immunomodulation that's particularly active on microglia and Treg balance. Stem cell therapy is largely an immune intervention in autism; the mechanism is paracrine immune modulation, not engraftment.

## PANS/PANDAS workup and treatment

If your child has sudden-onset OCD, tics, anxiety, sensory regression, or eating restriction — particularly post-infection — this is the PANS workup:

**Workup:**
- **Cunningham Panel** (Moleculera Biosciences) — anti-tubulin, anti-lysoganglioside, anti-D1, anti-D2, CaMKII activation
- **Strep titers** — ASO, anti-DNase B
- **Mycoplasma IgM/IgG**
- **Lyme panel** (igenex if standard panel negative)
- **Standard autoimmune** — ANA, RF, thyroid antibodies

**Treatment progression:**
1. NSAIDs (4-6 weeks initially)
2. Antibiotics if active infection
3. Antibiotic prophylaxis if recurrent
4. IVIG if moderate-severe and not responding
5. Plasmapheresis if severe and IVIG-refractory
6. LDN as long-term immune modulator
7. SSRIs for OCD (calibrated; some kids respond paradoxically)

**Specialty referral:**
- PANDAS Physicians Network (PPN) clinician finder
- Stanford PANS Clinic
- Mass General PANS Clinic
- Cohen PANDAS Center NYU

Researcher: [[Swedo_Sue]] (NIMH PANS founder), [[Cunningham_Madeleine]] (antibody panel)

## MCAS workup and treatment

If your child has chronic allergic / atopic disease + GI dysmotility + dysautonomia + flushing — this is the MCAS workup:

**Workup:**
- **Tryptase** (baseline + acute during flare; baseline elevation rare but specific)
- **Urinary methylhistamine** (24-hour collection)
- **Urinary prostaglandin D2 (PGD2 metabolites)**
- **Chromogranin A**
- **Cytokine panel**

**Treatment progression:**
1. H1 + H2 antihistamines (foundational)
2. Cromolyn sodium pre-meal
3. Ketotifen if needed
4. Quercetin + luteolin
5. Low-histamine diet
6. Avoidance of identified mast-cell triggers
7. Mast cell-aware allergist/immunologist consultation

Researcher: [[Theoharides_Theoharis]] (mast cell + microglial framework)

## Workup for general inflammatory presentation

If no specific PANS or MCAS picture but inflammatory features present:

- **CRP, ESR** — general inflammation
- **Cytokine panel** — IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-17, IL-10
- **Total immunoglobulins** — IgG subclasses, IgA, IgE
- **Complement** — C3, C4
- **Autoimmune basics** — ANA, RF, thyroid antibodies, anti-tTG (gluten)
- **Lyme panel** if outdoor exposure
- **Mycoplasma serology** if respiratory history

## Avoidance — what makes things worse

For inflammatory subgroup:
- **Glyphosate** (microbiome + immune disruption) — [[Seneff_Stephanie]]
- **Mold exposure** — chronic immune activator
- **Heavy metal exposure**
- **EBV / herpesvirus reactivation triggers** (chronic stress, immunosuppression)
- **Inflammatory diet** (sugar, refined seed oils, processed food)
- **Sleep deprivation** (immune modulator)

## Bottom line

Immune modulation interventions are highly effective in stratified subgroups but useless or counterproductive in non-inflammatory cases. The workup is essential before deploying. PANS/PANDAS in particular is a treatable autoimmune syndrome that is frequently misdiagnosed as autism — failing to recognize it means years of inappropriate behavioral intervention for what is actually an autoimmune condition.

If your child shows sudden-onset symptoms, post-infection regression, or any inflammatory features — start with the workup. The treatment ladder is well-established. Specialist clinical care exists.
