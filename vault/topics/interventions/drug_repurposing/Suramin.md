# Suramin — Antipurinergic Therapy for Autism

**Atlas record:** INT (low-dose suramin for autism, distinct from trypanosomiasis indication)
**Mechanism:** Non-selective purinergic receptor antagonist (P2X / P2Y); interrupts cell danger response (CDR)
**FDA approved for:** African trypanosomiasis (sleeping sickness) — original indication since 1920s
**Off-label autism use:** Concept-validated by Naviaux 2017 Phase I/II RCT; **not commercially available for autism in the US**

## Why this matters

[[Naviaux_Robert]]'s 3-Hit framework ([[SRC-001448 41902612]]) places persistent activation of the **cell danger response (CDR)** at the center of autism pathophysiology. CDR is initiated by extracellular ATP (eATP) acting on P2X / P2Y purinergic receptors. Persistent eATP signaling locks cells in a defensive metabolic state that, during the developmental window, prevents normal neurodevelopmental state transitions.

Suramin is a non-selective purinergic antagonist — it blocks both P2X and P2Y receptor families, plus several other ATP-binding sites. Theoretically, blocking eATP signaling should "release" the stuck CDR state and allow cells to resume normal metabolic and developmental trajectories.

This is the **mechanistic concept-validation** for the entire antipurinergic-therapy approach to autism. If suramin works (even partially, in a small RCT), it confirms the framework: persistent CDR is a real, modifiable contributor to autism phenotype.

## The Naviaux 2017 trial

**Citation:** Naviaux RK, Curtis B, Li K, Naviaux JC, Bright AT, Reiner GE, Westerfield M, Goh S, Alaynick WA, Wang L, Capparelli EV, Adams C, Sun J, Jain S, He F, Arellano DA, Mash LE, Reyes P, Hodgkin S, Bertrand AS. *"Low-dose suramin in autism spectrum disorder: a small, phase I/II, randomized clinical trial."* Annals of Clinical and Translational Neurology, 2017. [PMID 28695149](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28695149/).

**Design:**
- Randomized double-blind placebo-controlled
- N=10 boys with autism, ages 5-14
- Single intravenous infusion, 20 mg/kg
- Primary outcomes: ADOS-2 + Aberrant Behavior Checklist + Autism Diagnostic Interview-Revised + Repetitive Behavior Scale
- Followed for 6 weeks post-infusion

**Results:**
- Statistically significant improvements in core ASD domains (language, social interaction, repetitive behavior) within hours-to-days of infusion
- Effects waned over the 6-week follow-up window
- No serious adverse events at this dose
- Several children showed dramatic, immediate behavioral changes

**Critical caveats:**
- N=10 (small)
- Single infusion (durability not established for chronic dosing)
- Toxicity profile of chronic suramin administration is concerning — peripheral neuropathy, adrenal insufficiency, hematologic effects at higher doses or chronic exposure
- The dose used (20 mg/kg single) is "low-dose" *only* relative to historical trypanosomiasis dosing (which used multiple 20 mg/kg infusions totaling >100 mg/kg cumulative)

## Why suramin isn't widely used despite positive trial

Three reasons:

1. **Toxicity profile.** Suramin chronically causes peripheral neuropathy, adrenal cortical effects, hematologic abnormalities. The single-dose protocol used in autism is much safer than the chronic protocol used in trypanosomiasis, but no one has established a safe long-term repeated-dose protocol for autism.

2. **Regulatory pathway.** Suramin is FDA-orphan-drug for African trypanosomiasis only. There is no autism indication. Off-label pediatric use of an IV-only drug with serious side effects requires extraordinary clinical justification.

3. **Single-source supply.** Suramin is not commercially manufactured in the US. WHO maintains supplies for trypanosomiasis programs. Importing for autism use requires FDA expanded-access authorization on a case-by-case basis.

The result: suramin is a **proof-of-concept** drug, not a deployable therapy. Its value to the field is mechanistic — it validates that antipurinergic therapy can produce ASD-symptom improvement, motivating the search for safer purinergic modulators.

## What follows from this

**Next-generation antipurinergic therapies in development:**

- **Selective P2X7 antagonists** — narrower target than suramin; preclinical autism research
- **NTPDase / CD39 modulators** — alter eATP hydrolysis dynamics
- **Adenosine receptor modulators** — A1, A2A targeting
- **Dietary purine modulation** — investigational; substrate-level intervention

None of these are clinically available for autism yet. The entire field is downstream of Naviaux's suramin proof-of-concept.

## Practical note for parents

If you've heard about suramin in the autism community: it's real, the trial happened, the results were striking. But it's not a treatment you can pursue clinically in 2026. The take-away is the framework — that CDR / antipurinergic interventions are a real category — not that suramin specifically is something to seek out.

For families interested in CDR-related interventions that are clinically deployable today: focus on the **Naviaux 3-Hit framework** more broadly. That means [[topics/interventions/mitochondrial/00_MITOCHONDRIAL_OVERVIEW|mitochondrial cocktail support]] (CDR substrate optimization), avoidance of CDR-triggering exposures (per the [[Hannah Poling framework]] individual-level-conditional-risk model), and the broader anti-inflammatory + microbiome + methylation interventions that reduce ongoing CDR activation indirectly.
