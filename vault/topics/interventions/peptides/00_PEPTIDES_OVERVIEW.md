# 🧪 Peptides for Autism — Overview

Peptides are short chains of amino acids (typically 2-50) that signal cells, modulate neurotransmission, regulate inflammation, support tissue repair, or carry out specific biological functions. For autism, the peptide layer covers a broad spectrum from endogenous neuropeptides (oxytocin, vasopressin, IGF-1) to therapeutic peptides (BPC-157, Selank, Semax, Cerebrolysin) to gut-derived peptides (exorphins, gut peptides like PYY, GLP-1, ghrelin) to mitochondria-derived peptides (humanin, MOTS-c).

Some peptides have substantial peer-reviewed evidence (oxytocin, IGF-1). Others have minimal autism-specific evidence but strong mechanism (BPC-157). All require knowledgeable supervision.

## When to consider this class

Strong indication:
- **SHANK3 mutations / Phelan-McDermid syndrome** → IGF-1 (genotype-stratified)
- **Social cognition deficits as primary phenotype** → oxytocin (subgroup-positive in some trials)
- **Gut barrier dysfunction with food sensitivities** → exorphin biology + GFCF diet
- **Severe presentation, treatment-resistant** → cerebrolysin (Eastern European protocol)
- **Mitochondrial vulnerability** → mito peptides (humanin, MOTS-c — emerging)

### Endogenous neuropeptides (gold standard)

**[[topics/interventions/peptides/Oxytocin|Intranasal oxytocin]]** ([[INT-0061]] — csrs 53.7) *(deep-dive page in development)*
- Synthetic oxytocin delivered intranasally
- Modulates social cognition via amygdala/PFC
- Dose: 16-32 IU per dose, 1-2x daily
- Evidence: Multiple RCTs including Parker 2017 — positive in subset (effect heterogeneity per [[CLAUDE]] §9)
- Best evidence in low-baseline-oxytocin subgroup
- [[Hollander_Eric]] runs trials at Albert Einstein

**Vasopressin (AVP)** *(deep-dive page pending)*
- Closely related to oxytocin; complementary social-bonding mechanism
- Less studied than oxytocin in autism but emerging research
- Sex-differentiated effects (more relevant for males in some studies)

**IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor 1)** *(deep-dive page pending)*
- FDA-approved (Increlex) for primary IGFD; off-label for autism
- Best evidence in **SHANK3 mutations / Phelan-McDermid syndrome** — rescues synaptic protein synthesis defects
- [[Hollander_Eric]] Phase 2 trial work
- Genotype-stratified — most precise intervention currently available for monogenic autism

### Therapeutic peptides (synthetic / regenerative)

**BPC-157** ([[INT-0062]] — csrs 31.5) *(deep-dive page pending)*
- Pentadecapeptide derived from human gastric protective protein
- Strong preclinical anti-inflammatory + gut-protective + neuroprotective effects
- Dose: 200-500 mcg/day SC or oral
- No autism RCT — speculative mechanism strong, clinical evidence absent
- Use case: Gut barrier dysfunction, leaky gut, inflammation
- OTC (regulatory gray area in US — research compound)

**Selank** ([[INT-0063]] — csrs 28.7) *(deep-dive page pending)*
- Synthetic analog of tuftsin (immune-modulating peptide)
- Anxiolytic + nootropic in animal models
- Russian human studies show anxiolytic effect
- Dose: 300-900 mcg intranasal/day
- Speculative for autism specifically; established for anxiety

**Semax** ([[INT-0064]] — csrs 27.3) *(deep-dive page pending)*
- ACTH(4-10) analog
- Nootropic + BDNF-promoting in animal models
- Russian human studies show cognitive enhancement
- Dose: 400-1200 mcg intranasal/day
- Speculative for autism specifically

**Cerebrolysin** ([[INT-0065]] — csrs 60.8) *(deep-dive page pending)*
- Porcine brain-derived peptide mixture
- Trophic effects via neurotrophin-like activity
- Used widely in Eastern European pediatric autism protocols
- Dose: 1-5 mL IM, courses of 10-20 doses
- Pediatric autism trials: small N, generally positive
- Not FDA-approved in US; available offshore

### Gut-derived peptides

**Exorphins (gluteomorphin, casomorphin)** *(deep-dive page pending)*
- Opioid-like peptides derived from incomplete digestion of gluten and casein
- Biological substrate for the GFCF diet effect (per [[INT-0041]])
- Cross BBB in some children with intestinal hyperpermeability
- Mechanism: bind μ-opioid receptors in brain; can affect mood, behavior, sensory processing
- Reichelt + Knivsberg classical research; controversial but mechanistically intact
- See [[topics/interventions/nutrition_lifestyle/00_NUTRITION_OVERVIEW]] for GFCF detail

**Gut peptides — PYY, GLP-1, ghrelin** *(deep-dive page pending)*
- Endogenous peptides regulating satiety, gut-brain signaling, mood
- Increasingly studied in metabolic-psychiatric overlap (GLP-1 agonists for depression, etc.)
- Emerging research in autism / ADHD overlap
- Microbiome modulates production

**Antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) — defensins, cathelicidins** *(deep-dive page pending)*
- Endogenous antimicrobial peptides; first-line innate immunity
- Vitamin D drives cathelicidin expression
- Dysregulated in some autism subgroups
- Mechanism connection to barrier function and microbiome

### Mitochondria-derived peptides (emerging)

**Humanin** *(deep-dive page pending)*
- Small peptide encoded in mitochondrial 16S rRNA
- Anti-apoptotic, neuroprotective, anti-inflammatory
- Emerging research in neurodegeneration; minimal autism work yet
- Mechanism connection to [[Naviaux_Robert]] CDR framework

**MOTS-c** *(deep-dive page pending)*
- Mitochondrial-encoded peptide (12S rRNA)
- Metabolic regulation, exercise mimetic
- Emerging research; minimal autism-specific work
- Mechanism connection to mitochondrial-vulnerability subgroup

## How to use peptides

Most peptides require:
- **Prescription or research-grade compound** (varies by jurisdiction)
- **Knowledgeable physician** familiar with peptide therapy
- **Specific indication** based on genotype, biomarker, or strong clinical rationale
- **Monitoring** for response and side effects
- **Quality sourcing** — peptide quality varies dramatically

Peptide therapy clinics exist in functional medicine + anti-aging space. Quality varies.

## Workup before use

Indication-specific:

**For oxytocin:**
- Plasma oxytocin baseline (research labs)
- Behavioral baseline (social communication assessments)
- Sex differences matter for response prediction

**For IGF-1:**
- Genetic panel — SHANK3, PTEN, others — to identify candidates
- IGF-1 + IGFBP-3 plasma baseline
- Endocrinology consultation

**For BPC-157 (gut):**
- Stool panel (GI-MAP, Mosaic OAT)
- Gut barrier markers (zonulin, lipopolysaccharide)
- Food sensitivity panel

**For exorphins / GFCF:**
- Urinary peptides panel (some labs)
- Food sensitivity panel (IgG)
- Trial of GFCF diet for 6-12 weeks

**For mitochondrial peptides:**
- Mitochondrial workup ([[topics/interventions/mitochondrial/00_MITOCHONDRIAL_OVERVIEW]])

## Bottom line

The peptide layer is heterogeneous in evidence quality. **Strong evidence:** oxytocin (in subgroup), IGF-1 (in SHANK3+). **Mechanism but minimal autism evidence:** BPC-157, Selank, Semax. **Established but offshore:** Cerebrolysin. **Conceptually critical:** exorphin biology underlying GFCF response.

This section is in active development — per-peptide deep-dive pages being built out systematically per Session 3.5G. Until those are live, individual peptides are documented in their atlas INT-XXXX records and in the relevant overview pages above.

For a child with strong genetic finding (SHANK3) or strong gut/exorphin signature (food-sensitive, GI-symptomatic), this class becomes high-priority. For most other autistic children, peptides come after the foundational layers (microbiome, methylation, mitochondrial) have been worked.
