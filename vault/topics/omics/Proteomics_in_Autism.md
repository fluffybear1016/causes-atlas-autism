# Proteomics in Autism — The Functional Executor Layer

Proteomics measures the **complete set of proteins** in a cell, tissue, or biofluid, including their modifications (phosphorylation, glycosylation, ubiquitination, etc.). Proteins are the functional executors of genetic information — what genes are *for*, mechanistically. For autism research, proteomics is the layer where genetic and transcriptomic dysregulation translates into actual cellular function (or dysfunction).

## Why proteomics matters specifically

Several principles make proteomics critical for autism:

1. **Proteins do the work.** Genes encode; transcripts carry the message; proteins execute. Synaptic structure, neurotransmitter packaging and release, ion channel function, signaling cascades, mitochondrial energy production — all happen at the protein level. Genetic and transcriptomic findings only become biologically meaningful when traced to their protein consequences.

2. **Post-translational modifications matter intensely for neuronal function.** Phosphorylation status of kinases like CaMKII, ERK, mTOR, glycogen synthase kinase 3 (GSK3), and others changes second-by-second in active neurons. Static gene/RNA measurements miss this dynamic signaling layer. Phosphoproteomics resolves it.

3. **Protein abundance ≠ transcript abundance.** Multiple studies have shown that mRNA levels and protein levels correlate only modestly (Pearson r often 0.4–0.6). Translation efficiency, protein stability, post-translational regulation all decouple the two layers. Proteomics provides direct measurement of what's actually there.

4. **Synaptic proteins are the convergence point of autism risk.** SHANK family, NLGN, NRXN, PSD-95 (DLG4), Homer, glutamate receptors (GluN1/2A/2B), GABA receptors — the proteins encoded by high-confidence ASD genes are predominantly synaptic structural and signaling proteins. Proteomics measures their actual abundance and modification status.

5. **Mitochondrial proteomes connect to the [[Naviaux_Robert]] CDR framework.** Mitochondrial dysfunction in autism is detectable via proteomic changes in respiratory chain complexes, mitochondrial import/export machinery, and quality control proteins. This is a more functional readout than mitochondrial DNA copy number alone.

## What proteomics has shown about autism

### Brain proteomics

Postmortem ASD brain tissue proteomics consistently identifies:

- **Synaptic scaffolding deficits** — reduced PSD-95, Homer, SHANK3 protein abundance in cortical synapses, even when mRNA levels are less affected.
- **Mitochondrial respiratory complex abnormalities** — altered abundance of complex I, III, IV subunits in autism prefrontal cortex.
- **Cytoskeletal alterations** — actin-associated proteins, microtubule regulators (MAP2, tau-related proteins) showing altered abundance.
- **Astrocyte and microglial markers** — GFAP, IBA1, and complement proteins (C1q, C3) elevated, consistent with neuroinflammation framework.
- **Synaptic vesicle and presynaptic machinery** — synaptophysin, synaptobrevin, syntaxin abundance changes.

### Phosphoproteomics — the dynamic signaling layer

Phosphoproteomic studies have identified dysregulated phosphorylation in:

- **mTOR pathway** — phospho-S6K1, phospho-4E-BP1, phospho-S6 — central to autism syndromes from TSC1/2, PTEN, FMR1 (Fragile X protein normally restrains synaptic protein synthesis via mTOR-related signaling).
- **MAPK / ERK signaling** — neuronal activity-dependent phosphorylation cascade.
- **CaMKII** — calcium/calmodulin-dependent kinase II — central to synaptic plasticity.
- **GSK3** — Wnt signaling kinase, intersects with SOX7 / Wnt pathway findings ([[SRC-001458 40373085]]).

Multiple pharmacological autism interventions target this phosphoproteomic layer — rapamycin (mTOR inhibitor) directly modulates phospho-S6K1; bumetanide indirectly affects calcium-driven phosphoproteomic networks via NKCC1.

### Plasma / serum proteomics — accessible biomarkers

Multiple studies have identified differential plasma proteins in autism. Findings include:

- **Inflammatory proteins** — elevated CRP, IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α in subgroups (consistent with [[Theoharides_Theoharis]] mast cell/microglial framework).
- **Complement proteins** — C3, C4 elevations.
- **Amyloid and prion-related proteins** — under investigation as potential biomarkers.
- **Insulin-like growth factor (IGF) family** — relevant to [[Hollander_Eric]]'s IGF-1 SHANK3 trial framework.
- **Cytokine signatures** — distinguishing inflammatory subgroup from non-inflammatory.

### Stool / mucosal proteomics

Recent multi-omics integration ([[SRC-001455 39870302]] Osama 2025 J Adv Res) measures host fecal proteome alongside microbiomic data. Findings:

- **Mucin-13 (MUC13)** — barrier glycoprotein, downregulated in autistic children, consistent with intestinal barrier compromise.
- **Kallikrein (KLK1)** and **transthyretin (TTR)** — upregulated, consistent with altered mucosal immunity.
- **Neutrophil elastase** and **myeloperoxidase** — downregulated.

This is the integration of proteomics into the gut-brain axis literature — proteomics provides the host side of the host-microbe interaction picture.

## Proteomics in clinical practice

In 2026, clinical proteomic testing for autism is **emerging but not standard**. Available options:

- **Cytokine / inflammatory panel** — multiplex serum cytokine measurement is widely available and clinically actionable. Useful for stratifying inflammatory vs non-inflammatory subgroups.
- **Tryptase + chromogranin A + methylhistamine** — mast cell activation panel; relevant for [[Theoharides_Theoharis]] MCAS subgroup.
- **Cunningham Panel** — anti-neuronal antibody panel; technically a proteomic panel measuring antibody binding to specific neuronal antigens. See [[Cunningham_Madeleine]].
- **Comprehensive proteomic profiling** — research-grade, not clinically standardized for autism. Available through some commercial labs (Olink, SomaLogic platforms) on a parent-paid basis.
- **Allergen-specific IgE / IgG panels** — proteomic readouts of immune sensitization, relevant for inflammatory subgroups.

Like transcriptomics, the most clinically-actionable proteomic findings in 2026 reach the bedside indirectly — through their downstream consequences in metabolomic, microbiomic, and immune workups, rather than through direct comprehensive proteomic profiling.
