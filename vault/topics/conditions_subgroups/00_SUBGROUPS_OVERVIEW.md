# 🏥 Conditions and Subgroups That Overlap With Autism

Many children carry an autism diagnosis but actually have a more specific condition that's clinically separable, biomarker-defined, and often treatable in ways the general "autism" framework doesn't capture. This section catalogs those subgroups.

**Recognizing these subgroups matters intensely** — many children languish for years in autism-services frameworks when their actual condition is PANS/PANDAS, MCAS, mitochondrial disease, or a specific genetic syndrome that has its own treatment paradigm.

## Subgroups in development

These subgroups have meaningful evidence bases and merit their own deep-dive pages. Per CLAUDE.md Sessions 3.5C and 3.5D roadmaps, atlas-level hypothesis entries are also planned.

### 1. PANS / PANDAS *(deep-dive page pending)*

**Pediatric Acute-onset Neuropsychiatric Syndrome / Pediatric Autoimmune Neuropsychiatric Disorders Associated with Streptococcus**

A real, identifiable autoimmune subgroup of children whose neuropsychiatric symptoms (often labeled "autism" or "regressive autism") are post-infectious and immunologically driven.

**Recognition:**
- Sudden onset OCD, tics, anxiety, sensory regression, eating restriction
- Episodic / waxing-waning course
- Symptoms often start after an infection (strep, Mycoplasma, Lyme, viral)

**Workup:**
- [[Cunningham_Madeleine]]'s Cunningham Panel (anti-tubulin, anti-lysoganglioside, anti-D1, anti-D2, CaMKII activation)
- Strep titers (ASO, anti-DNase B)
- Mycoplasma + Lyme panels
- Standard autoimmune panel

**Treatment:**
- NSAIDs (first-line, 4-6 weeks)
- Antibiotics for active infection
- Antibiotic prophylaxis for recurrent
- IVIG for moderate-severe cases
- Plasmapheresis for severe IVIG-refractory
- LDN for chronic immune modulation
- See [[topics/interventions/immune_modulation/00_IMMUNE_OVERVIEW]] for treatment detail

**Researchers:** [[Swedo_Sue]] (NIMH founder of PANDAS framework), [[Cunningham_Madeleine]] (antibody panel)

**Specialty centers:** Stanford PANS Clinic, Mass General PANS Clinic, Cohen PANDAS Center NYU, PANDAS Physicians Network clinician finder

### 2. MCAS (Mast Cell Activation Syndrome) *(deep-dive page pending)*

A real, treatable subgroup of children with mast cell hyperactivity affecting multiple organ systems including brain.

**Recognition:**
- Chronic allergic / atopic disease (eczema, asthma, food allergies)
- GI dysmotility, abdominal pain, bloating
- Dysautonomia (heart rate / blood pressure / temperature variability)
- Flushing, hives, intermittent rashes
- Sleep disturbance
- Cognitive features ("brain fog")

**Workup:**
- Tryptase (baseline + during flare)
- Urinary methylhistamine (24-hour collection)
- Urinary prostaglandin D2 metabolites
- Chromogranin A
- Cytokine panel

**Treatment:**
- H1 + H2 antihistamines (foundational)
- Cromolyn sodium pre-meal
- Ketotifen
- Quercetin + luteolin (mast cell + microglial stabilizers)
- Low-histamine diet
- See [[topics/interventions/immune_modulation/00_IMMUNE_OVERVIEW]] for treatment detail

**Researcher:** [[Theoharides_Theoharis]] (mast cell + microglial framework; luteolin)

### 3. Methylation Biotypes (Walsh framework) *(deep-dive page pending)*

[[Walsh_William]]'s biotype classification subdivides methylation issues into clinically meaningful subtypes:

**Undermethylator phenotype (~30-40% of autism)**
- Low whole-blood histamine
- Low SAM/SAH ratio
- Personality cluster: high inner tension, perfectionism, OCD features, seasonal allergies
- Treatment: Methyl donors (methionine, SAM-e, methyl-B12)

**Overmethylator phenotype (~5-10% of autism)**
- High whole-blood histamine
- High SAM/SAH ratio
- Personality cluster: anxious, low motivation, paradoxical histamine elevation
- Treatment: Folate-cautious; niacinamide, B6

**Pyroluria / Kryptopyrroluria phenotype**
- Elevated urinary kryptopyrroles
- Stress intolerance, anxiety, mood lability, poor short-term memory
- Treatment: B6 (P5P), zinc, GLA, magnesium

**Copper:Zinc imbalance phenotype**
- Elevated free copper, low zinc
- Hyperactivity, sensory issues, behavioral dysregulation
- Treatment: Zinc supplementation, molybdenum, metallothionein-promoting protocols

See [[topics/interventions/methylation_pathway/00_METHYLATION_OVERVIEW]] for treatment integration.

### 4. Mitochondrial Disease / Dysfunction *(deep-dive page pending)*

Per [[Rossignol_Daniel]] + [[Naviaux_Robert]] frameworks, ~30-50% of autism has mitochondrial involvement.

**Recognition:**
- Regression after illness, fever, vaccination, surgery
- Low muscle tone (hypotonia)
- Fatigue / low endurance
- Multiple regressions or plateaus
- Family history of mitochondrial disease (especially maternal)
- GI dysmotility

**Workup:**
- Lactate, pyruvate, L:P ratio
- Acylcarnitine profile
- Creatine kinase
- Mosaic OAT (citric acid cycle metabolites)

**Treatment:**
- Mitochondrial cocktail (CoQ10, L-carnitine, B-vitamins, antioxidants)
- Avoidance of mitotoxic medications (valproate, statins, certain antibiotics)
- Life-stage-specific protocols
- See [[topics/interventions/mitochondrial/00_MITOCHONDRIAL_OVERVIEW]]

**Researcher:** [[Rossignol_Daniel]], [[Naviaux_Robert]]

### 5. Cerebral Folate Deficiency / FRAA-Positive Subgroup *(deep-dive page pending)*

The leucovorin-responsive subgroup. ~70-75% of autistic children test positive for folate receptor alpha autoantibodies (FRAAs).

**Recognition:**
- Language delay or regression
- Behavioral irritability
- Family history of MTHFR variants

**Workup:**
- FRAA antibody panel (Religious Sisters of Mercy / Ramaekers lab; some commercial labs)
- CSF folate measurement (gold standard but invasive)
- Plasma folate

**Treatment:**
- Leucovorin (folinic acid) trial — see [[topics/interventions/methylation_pathway/00_METHYLATION_OVERVIEW]]

**Researcher:** [[Frye_Richard]] — *The Folate Fix* book + 20+ years primary literature

### 6. Genetic Syndromic Autism *(deep-dive page pending)*

Children with identified rare large-effect variants who carry autism diagnosis but actually have a specific genetic syndrome with its own treatment paradigm.

**Includes:**
- **Fragile X (FMR1)** — most common single-gene cause; mGluR5 modulator research; specific medical surveillance needs
- **Tuberous sclerosis (TSC1/TSC2)** — mTOR pathway; rapamycin response
- **Phelan-McDermid syndrome (SHANK3)** — IGF-1 trial-stratified; [[Hollander_Eric]]
- **PTEN hamartoma syndrome** — mTOR pathway; rapamycin response
- **Rett syndrome (MECP2)** — distinct condition though sometimes labeled autism initially
- **Angelman syndrome (UBE3A)** — high autism comorbidity
- **15q11-q13 duplication syndrome**
- **CHD8 variants** — distinct phenotype with macrocephaly

See [[topics/omics/Genomics_of_Autism]] for genetic workup and gene-family detail.

### 7. Regressive Autism (Hannah Poling framework) *(deep-dive page pending)*

Children who developed apparently normally and then lost acquired skills following an environmental trigger.

**Recognition:**
- Documented loss of acquired developmental skills
- Temporal correlation with infection, vaccination, fever, or other physiological challenge
- Pre-trigger developmental milestones intact

**Mechanism per [[Hannah Poling framework]]:**
- Susceptibility (often mitochondrial vulnerability) × Trigger → CDR activation → developmental state-transition disruption

**Treatment priorities:**
- Comprehensive multi-omics workup
- Mitochondrial support
- Cell therapy candidate (per [[topics/interventions/cell_therapy/00_CELL_THERAPY_OVERVIEW]])
- Anti-inflammatory / immune modulation

**Federal precedent:** [[SRC-001418 Hannah Poling federal Vaccine Court 2008 ruling]]

## How to use this section

If your child carries an autism diagnosis but ANY of these subgroup features, this section is the priority:

| Subgroup | Hot question |
|---|---|
| PANS/PANDAS | "Did symptoms start suddenly?" |
| MCAS | "Are there allergic / GI / dysautonomia features?" |
| Methylation biotypes | "Does the child fit Walsh's personality clusters?" |
| Mitochondrial | "Was there regression after illness or fever?" |
| FRAA-positive | "Is language the dominant issue?" |
| Syndromic | "Are there multi-organ involvement or dysmorphic features?" |
| Regressive | "Did the child lose acquired skills?" |

Misclassifying any of these as "general autism" leaves treatable disease untreated. This is one of the most underappreciated framework problems in mainstream pediatrics.
