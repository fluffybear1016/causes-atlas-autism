# The 7 Autism Phenotypes — Neurobiochemistry & Biophysics

Autism is not one condition. The atlas resolves it into **7 mechanistically-distinct phenotypes** ([[PHE-0001]] through [[PHE-0007]]) plus 4 [[topics/conditions_subgroups/Walsh_Biotypes|Walsh biotypes]] ([[PHE-0008]] through [[PHE-0011]]). Each of the original 7 is defined by a different pathway-level lesion — and each has a distinct neurobiochemical and biophysical signature.

This is not a taxonomic exercise. **Knowing the phenotype determines the intervention bundle.** A child with [[PHE-0001 Cerebral folate deficiency phenotype|cerebral folate deficiency]] needs leucovorin; a child with [[PHE-0007 GABA_Cl- imbalance phenotype|GABA/Cl- imbalance]] needs bumetanide; the same intervention applied to the wrong phenotype produces the noise that has plagued every unstratified autism RCT in history.

## Pathway-level taxonomy

These phenotypes are not orthogonal — they share substrate. The atlas captures cross-talk:

- **Folate ↔ methylation ↔ mitochondria**: [[PHE-0001]] and [[PHE-0002]] share substrate dependency on B-vitamin cofactors and one-carbon metabolism. Many children meet partial criteria for both.
- **Immune ↔ mitochondria ↔ CDR**: [[PHE-0003]] (immune) and [[PHE-0002]] (mito) couple via [[Naviaux_Robert]]'s cell danger response. Persistent CDR locks both immune and mitochondrial cells in defensive state.
- **Gut ↔ immune ↔ brain**: [[PHE-0004]] (microbiome) feeds [[PHE-0003]] (immune) via LPS translocation, microbial neurotoxin (4-cresol, HPHPA) production, and gut-derived inflammation crossing BBB.
- **mTOR ↔ Fragile X ↔ E:I**: [[PHE-0005]] (mTOR) and [[PHE-0006]] (FMR1) both converge on dendritic protein synthesis dysregulation. mGluR5 hyperactivation in FXS feeds excessive mTOR signaling locally.
- **GABA ↔ mitochondria ↔ chloride**: [[PHE-0007]] (GABA/Cl-) depends on NKCC1/KCC2 expression, which is regulated by mitochondrial energy status (ATP-dependent ion pumping).

A given child often shows partial features of 2-3 phenotypes simultaneously. The clinical workup is biomarker-driven phenotype profiling, not "pick one."

## Neurobiochemistry vs biophysics

Each phenotype deep-dive maps two layers:

**Neurobiochemistry** — the molecular lesion: which enzyme, transporter, receptor, metabolite, or pathway is disrupted; which neurotransmitter system is affected; which cofactor or substrate is depleted; which post-translational modification is dysregulated.

**Biophysics** — the cellular and circuit-level consequence: how synaptic transmission is altered; how ion gradients shift; how excitation-inhibition balance changes; how network oscillations re-organize; how blood-brain-barrier permeability changes; what EEG / MEG / fMRI signatures result.

These two layers are coupled — biochemistry sets the stage for biophysics — but distinguishing them is essential for clinical reasoning. A biochemical lesion (folate transport block) produces a downstream biophysical consequence (synaptic plasticity deficit) which manifests as a clinical phenotype (developmental regression with motor + cognitive features).
