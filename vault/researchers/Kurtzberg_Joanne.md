# Joanne Kurtzberg, MD — Pediatric stem cell therapy lead at Duke

Joanne Kurtzberg is the **Jerome S. Harris Professor of Pediatrics at Duke**, the **Director and Chief Scientific Officer of the Robertson Clinical and Translational Cell Therapy Program**, and the **Director of the Marcus Center for Cellular Cures**. She is one of the foundational figures in pediatric cord-blood cellular therapy. She built and leads the Duke pediatric stem cell program — the highest-volume and most rigorously-published academic stem-cell-for-pediatric-neurological-conditions program in the United States. With autism research collaborator [[Dawson_Geraldine]] (Duke Center for Autism), her group has produced the Phase 1 ([[SRC-001450 28378499]]) and Phase 2 RCT ([[SRC-001451 32444220]]) cord blood autism trials that constitute the gold-standard peer-reviewed evidence base for stem cell therapy in autism.

## Why she matters

Stem cell therapy for autism could easily have remained a fringe / commercial-clinic intervention with no rigorous evidence base. Kurtzberg's program is the reason it didn't — she and Dawson built the trial infrastructure, ran the Phase 1, ran the Phase 2 RCT, and published the results in mainstream peer-reviewed journals. The fact that we now know that **autologous cord blood is safe in young autistic children, that it produces measurable behavioral signal in a stratifiable subgroup, and that the inflammatory subset is the responder population** is because of Kurtzberg's program. Without it the field would still be operating on case reports and offshore clinic testimonials.

She is also one of the foundational figures in the broader pediatric cord-blood-banking infrastructure. Her work establishing public cord blood banking in the US has made allogeneic cord blood therapy possible for thousands of children with cancer, immunodeficiencies, metabolic disorders, and (now) neurological indications.

## Key work

**Duke Pediatric Cord Blood Transplant Program** (founded 1990s, ongoing) — One of the world's largest pediatric cord blood transplant centers. Hundreds of patients per year across cancer, metabolic, immunodeficiency, and neurological indications.

**[[SRC-001450 28378499]] — Dawson, Sun, Davlantis, … Kurtzberg 2017** *Stem Cells Translational Medicine.* Phase 1 autologous cord blood for autism. 25 children, single-center open-label, established safety + behavioral signal. The foundational autism-cord-blood paper.

**[[SRC-001451 32444220]] — Dawson, Sun, Baker, … Kurtzberg 2020** *Journal of Pediatrics.* Phase 2 RCT autologous cord blood vs placebo for autism. 180 children, 6-month primary outcome, formally negative on the primary outcome but with critical subgroup signal in non-anxious / normal-IQ / inflammatory responders.

**Allogeneic cord blood + cord tissue MSC trials** — Subsequent and ongoing trials extending beyond autologous cord blood (which is only available to families who banked at birth) into allogeneic products that can scale to broader populations.

**Cerebral palsy cord blood trials** — Parallel program with similar methodology, demonstrating safety + signal in cerebral palsy subgroups.

**Marcus Center for Cellular Cures** — Major institutional commitment by Duke to pediatric cellular therapy across multiple indications. Kurtzberg is the Director.

**Active 2023–2026** — Continuing trials, expanding cell-therapy indications, advancing toward late-stage trials in stratified subgroups.

## Concept summary

**Autologous cord blood as starting point.** Kurtzberg's strategy was methodologically sound: start with autologous cord blood (the child's own banked-at-birth cells), which has zero rejection risk and minimal regulatory complexity. Establish safety. Establish signal. Then expand to allogeneic protocols where the methodology is more complex but the population reach is vastly larger.

**Stratified Phase 2 design.** The Dawson 2020 Phase 2 RCT pre-specified subgroup analyses by IQ status, anxiety baseline, and inflammatory markers. That pre-specification is what made the subgroup signal a methodologically valid finding rather than post-hoc fishing. The field's most important takeaway from this trial — that the responder subgroup is identifiable from baseline characteristics — depends on that subgroup-stratified design.

**Mechanism agnosticism.** Kurtzberg's group is careful not to overclaim the mechanism. The cells don't engraft. The therapeutic effect is paracrine. Whether the dominant mechanism is immunomodulation, neurotrophic signaling, mitochondrial transfer, or something else is still being characterized. The therapeutic effect, where present, is real even if the precise mechanism is incompletely characterized.

## Practical takeaway for parents

The Duke pediatric stem cell program at the Marcus Center for Cellular Cures is the gold-standard pathway for stem cell therapy in autism. They run the most rigorous trials, have the most rigorous safety oversight, and have the most published outcome data. Trial enrollment is competitive and protocol-defined.

For a family seriously considering stem cell therapy for an autistic child, the order of operations is: (1) check Duke ClinicalTrials.gov listings for currently-enrolling autism cord blood or cord tissue MSC trials; (2) if eligible, enroll there first as the lowest-cost and highest-quality option; (3) only if not eligible, consider Tier 2 alternatives like Stem Cell Institute Panama under [[Riordan_Neil]].
