# Manuel Casanova, MD — Cortical minicolumns and TMS therapy

Manuel Casanova is one of the foundational neuroanatomists of autism. His core empirical contribution is the documentation, in postmortem autistic brain tissue, that **autistic brains have abnormally narrow and densely-packed cortical minicolumns** — the basic vertical processing units of the cerebral cortex — and that this anatomical signature is consistent across age, sex, and severity within the autism population. His clinical translation of this finding is the application of **low-frequency repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS)** as an experimental therapy that modulates the cortical excitation/inhibition balance these minicolumns set.

## Why he matters

Casanova's minicolumn work is one of the most important neuroanatomical findings in autism research. It connects cellular-level brain anatomy to the macro-level "connectivity disorder" framing of autism (local hyperconnectivity, long-distance hypoconnectivity), and provides a tissue-level explanation for the sensory hypersensitivity and altered cognitive processing that characterizes the phenotype. Several of the atlas's mechanism-layer hypotheses around excitatory/inhibitory balance (see [[MEC-0020]], [[HYP-0073]]) connect directly to this minicolumn biology.

## Key work

**Casanova et al. (multiple, 2002–present)** — Postmortem brain tissue analysis demonstrating reduced minicolumn width and increased density in autism. Published in major neurology journals over more than two decades.

**rTMS protocol for autism** — Casanova's group developed and clinically tested low-frequency rTMS (typically over dorsolateral prefrontal cortex) as a treatment that reduces cortical noise and improves attention, executive function, and reduces repetitive behaviors. Multiple peer-reviewed trials.

**Cortical Chauvinism (Substack)** — Long-running personal blog where Casanova writes accessible long-form pieces translating his and others' autism neuroscience for parents and informed readers. One of the most substantive autism-neuroscience public-facing communications in the field.

**Active 2023–2026** — Continuing rTMS research, reviews, and public-facing writing.

## Concept summary

**Minicolumns as the fundamental cortical computation unit.** A cortical minicolumn is a vertical column of approximately 80–120 neurons spanning all cortical layers — the basic processing unit of the cerebral cortex. Mountcastle established their importance in primate neurology decades ago. Casanova's contribution is that autism shows a specific anatomical signature: more minicolumns, more densely packed, with reduced lateral inhibition.

**Excitation/inhibition balance.** The narrow tightly-packed minicolumns translate to reduced GABAergic lateral inhibition and elevated cortical noise. This connects directly to the GABA-developmental-switch hypothesis ([[HYP-0071]]), the bumetanide intervention story ([[Ben-Ari_Yehezkel]]), and the broader excitation/inhibition imbalance framework.

**Connectivity disorder.** The minicolumn signature predicts local hyperconnectivity and long-distance hypoconnectivity — the specific connectivity pattern documented in fMRI and DTI studies of autism.

**TMS as therapeutic.** Low-frequency rTMS reduces cortical excitability over targeted regions. Casanova's protocol (typically weekly sessions over dorsolateral prefrontal cortex) has shown improvements in executive function, attention, and reduction of repetitive/stereotyped behaviors in autism. Effect sizes are subgroup-dependent (consistent with [[CLAUDE]] §9 effect heterogeneity principle).

## Practical takeaway for parents

For families considering rTMS as an autism intervention:

- It is experimental but has multiple peer-reviewed trials supporting safety and modest efficacy.
- Session protocols are typically weekly to twice-weekly over weeks-to-months.
- Best documented effect: improvements in executive function and reduction of repetitive/stereotyped behaviors. Less documented effect on core social communication.
- Several autism-experienced TMS clinics exist in the US; Casanova's group at University of South Carolina is the most research-active.

For families interested in the underlying neuroscience, **Cortical Chauvinism** (Casanova's Substack) is one of the very few accessible long-form autism-neuroanatomy resources at this depth.
