# Prof. Hideo Honda

**Affiliation:** Department of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry, Yamanashi University Faculty of Medicine, Japan

## Why this researcher matters

Honda's 2005 paper in *J Child Psychol Psychiatry* is a **rare natural-experiment design** in autism epidemiology. After Japan **withdrew the MMR vaccine in 1993**, Honda's group studied autism incidence in defined birth cohorts before and after withdrawal in Yokohama. The study found that autism incidence **continued to rise after MMR withdrawal**, providing population-level evidence inconsistent with a simple MMR → autism population-average causal claim.

The study is methodologically important because:

- Natural-experiment designs are unusually rigorous in observational epidemiology
- It uses **defined birth cohorts** before/after a clean exposure change
- The cohort had standardized autism ascertainment

It is the source already in the atlas as [[SRC-001421]], and one of the most-cited population-average studies on the MMR question.

## Key contributions

### Honda 2005 — MMR withdrawal natural experiment

After Japan withdrew the MMR triple vaccine in 1993 due to high rates of aseptic meningitis associated with the Urabe mumps strain, Honda's group documented:

- **Autism incidence in Yokohama** in cohorts born 1988-1996
- Incidence was **rising** before MMR withdrawal
- Incidence **continued to rise** after MMR was no longer administered
- The cumulative incidence ratio in MMR-exposed vs unexposed cohorts did not show an MMR-specific signal

The conclusion at population-average level: MMR is unlikely to be a meaningful population-average driver of autism incidence trends.

### Per atlas epistemic principles

Per [[CLAUDE]] §6 and §9, this study answers a **population-average question** — specifically whether MMR is a population-driver of autism. It is methodologically powerful for that question. It does **not** address whether MMR could trigger autism in a [[Hannah Poling framework|mitochondrially-vulnerable individual subset]] — population-average designs cannot resolve subgroup-specific effects.

This is consistent with the Hannah Poling framework: a vaccine that has minor population-average risk can be catastrophic for a child with the relevant susceptibility profile (mitochondrial vulnerability, immune dysregulation). The Honda 2005 result and the Hannah Poling 2008 federal ruling are **complementary rather than contradictory**.

## Notes

The Yokohama cohort design is unique because Japanese local government health systems track all childhood vaccinations and developmental assessments — generating natural cohorts that other countries' fragmented health systems can rarely replicate. Honda's collaborator on the 2005 paper, **Sir Michael Rutter**, was one of the most important British autism researchers of the 20th century.
