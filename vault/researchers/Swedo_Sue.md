# Sue Swedo, MD — PANS/PANDAS and the post-infectious autoimmune subgroup

Sue Swedo is the pediatrician and NIMH researcher who **discovered and named PANDAS** — Pediatric Autoimmune Neuropsychiatric Disorders Associated with Streptococcal infections — and led the diagnostic-criteria development for the broader **PANS** (Pediatric Acute-onset Neuropsychiatric Syndrome) framework. Her work establishes a real, identifiable autoimmune subgroup of children whose neuropsychiatric symptoms (including autism regression in some cases) are post-infectious and immunologically driven.

## Why she matters

A subset of children diagnosed with autism — particularly regressive-course autism with sudden behavioral / sensory / OCD features — actually meet PANS or PANDAS criteria and respond to immune-targeted intervention rather than (or in addition to) standard autism therapies. Without Swedo's diagnostic framework, these children get caught in the autism diagnostic bucket and miss the immune workup that would identify their actual treatable syndrome.

The atlas's planned [[Session 3.5C]] expansion adds PANS/PANDAS as a hypothesis layer specifically to capture this subgroup.

## Key work

**Swedo et al. 1998** — Original PANDAS criteria paper. *American Journal of Psychiatry*. Established the syndrome as a recognized diagnosis: pediatric onset, abrupt symptom onset following streptococcal infection, neurologic abnormalities, episodic course.

**PANS criteria (2012)** — Expansion beyond strep-specific PANDAS to PANS, which captures the same acute-onset autoimmune neuropsychiatric syndrome triggered by any of multiple infectious / inflammatory triggers (Mycoplasma, Borrelia / Lyme, viral, environmental). Diagnostic criteria established by international consensus working group with Swedo as lead investigator.

**Cunningham Panel** — Lab test for anti-neuronal antibodies (anti-tubulin, anti-lysoganglioside, anti-dopamine D1, anti-dopamine D2, CaMKII). Developed in collaboration with Madeleine Cunningham. The biomarker that operationalizes PANS/PANDAS diagnosis and predicts response to immune therapy.

**NIMH research portfolio (extensive)** — Decades of NIH-funded research establishing PANS/PANDAS as a real, mechanistically-grounded syndrome. This is one of the few areas where mainstream academic NIMH-funded research and the functional-medicine clinical community are largely aligned.

**Current status (2023–2026)** — Swedo continues to advise clinical groups, present at conferences, and consult on diagnostic criteria. Multiple academic centers (Stanford PANS Clinic, Harvard / Mass General, Cohen PANDAS clinic at NYU, etc.) now offer formal PANS/PANDAS diagnostic and treatment programs.

## Concept summary

**Acute symptom onset.** PANS/PANDAS presents with sudden, dramatic onset of OCD, tic, anxiety, sensory, behavioral, sleep, and eating symptoms — often within days. This abruptness is the diagnostic anchor that distinguishes it from classical autism (which develops over months) and from neurodevelopmental regression (which is typically more gradual).

**Post-infectious autoimmune mechanism.** Anti-neuronal antibodies cross-react with basal ganglia structures, particularly the striatum. This is a mimicry mechanism — antibodies generated against the infection (often *Streptococcus*) cross-react with neuronal antigens.

**Treatment is immune-targeted.** Antibiotics for active infection. NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) as first-line anti-inflammatory. IVIG (intravenous immunoglobulin) for moderate cases. Plasmapheresis for severe cases. Low-dose naltrexone (LDN) for chronic immune modulation. SSRIs for OCD symptom management. Antibiotic prophylaxis in PANDAS.

**Overlap with autism.** A meaningful percentage of children diagnosed with regressive autism — especially those with sudden-onset presentations, OCD features, eating restriction, separation anxiety, sensory regression — actually have PANS/PANDAS or have it as a comorbidity. This subset responds dramatically to immune intervention and is functionally a different syndrome from what mainstream autism care addresses.

## Practical takeaway for parents

If your child has had:

- Sudden onset of OCD, tics, anxiety, eating restriction, sensory regression
- Symptoms that started after an illness (strep, Mycoplasma, Lyme, viral)
- An episodic / waxing-waning course
- Significant behavioral regression from a previously higher functional baseline

— get the **Cunningham Panel** + ASO / anti-DNase B (strep titers) + Mycoplasma IgM/IgG + Lyme panel + standard autoimmune workup. A PANS/PANDAS-experienced specialist (PANDAS Physicians Network has a clinician finder; Stanford PANS Clinic, Cohen PANDAS at NYU, and the Mass General PANS Clinic are referral centers) is the right consult.

This is one of the most under-diagnosed treatable causes of "regressive autism" symptoms in pediatric medicine. The diagnostic workup is straightforward. Missing it can mean years of behavioral therapy for what is actually an autoimmune syndrome that responds to immune-targeted treatment.
