# Wendy Chung, MD, PhD — SPARK and population-scale autism genetics

Wendy Chung is the **Chief of the Department of Pediatrics at Boston Children's Hospital / Harvard Medical School** (named to the role February 2023) and serves as Director of Clinical Research at the Simons Foundation Autism Research Initiative (SFARI), where she leads **SPARK** (Simons Foundation Powering Autism Research for Knowledge), the largest autism genetic cohort in the world. Her contribution to the atlas is the **population-scale infrastructure** that has made systematic autism gene discovery possible.

## Why she matters

The SFARI Gene database that powers the atlas's [[HYP-0028 Inherited polygenic risk]] hypothesis (currently 1,564 genes in the atlas, with 781 SFARI Tier 1+2 genes wired through) is built from cohort-scale sequencing data. SPARK is the cohort that produced much of that data. Without Chung-class infrastructure, the genetic substrate layer of the atlas would not exist.

Like [[Geschwind_Daniel]], Chung operates in mainstream genetics-forward autism research. The atlas integrates this work as one substrate layer of a multifactorial framework rather than treating it as the primary or sufficient causal explanation.

## Key work

**SPARK cohort** — 100,000+ autistic individuals and family members sequenced. The largest single autism genetics resource in existence. Has produced multiple high-impact gene-discovery papers.

**Earlier work on autism gene discovery** — Multiple landmark papers identifying high-confidence (Tier 1) autism genes through SPARK-class sequencing. These are the genes the atlas wires to [[HYP-0028]].

**Chief of Pediatrics, Boston Children's Hospital (2023–)** — Leading the Department of Pediatrics at one of the world's premier pediatric academic medical centers; her position influences the broader research and clinical infrastructure for autism and pediatric genetics. Continues to direct SFARI clinical research (SPARK + Searchlight cohorts) in parallel.

**Active ongoing research portfolio** — SPARK enrollment continues; rare-variant discovery work continues.

## Concept summary

**Cohort-scale gene discovery.** The combinatorial space of rare variants in autism is too large to discover with small cohorts. SPARK-scale infrastructure is what enables identification of statistically robust gene-trait associations.

**Polygenic risk scoring.** Common-variant polygenic risk scores derived from SPARK and similar cohorts allow quantitative estimates of inherited risk. This is a tool that, combined with the [[Hannah Poling framework]], should eventually feed into the personalized risk calculator the atlas is building toward (see [[Session 4]]).

**Family-based design.** SPARK enrolls families, not just individuals, which enables de novo variant discovery and inheritance-pattern analysis.
