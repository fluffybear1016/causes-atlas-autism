# Prof. Christopher Gillberg

**Affiliation:** Senior Professor of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry, Gothenburg University; founder, Gillberg Neuropsychiatry Centre (GNC)

## Why this researcher matters

Gillberg is one of the most prolific autism researchers in history — author of >700 peer-reviewed papers spanning four decades of autism epidemiology, phenotype description, and clinical recognition. He coined the term **ESSENCE** (Early Symptomatic Syndromes Eliciting Neurodevelopmental Clinical Examinations) — the framework that transformed European clinical recognition of autism away from siloed diagnostic categories toward an integrated neurodevelopmental view.

## Key contributions

### The ESSENCE framework

Gillberg's ESSENCE concept (2010) recognized that early-childhood neurodevelopmental concerns — autism, ADHD, intellectual disability, motor coordination, tics, language disorders, learning differences, eating problems — almost always co-occur and should be evaluated holistically rather than referred to siloed specialists.

Clinical implication: a child presenting with one ESSENCE feature has a high probability of having or developing another. Pediatric workup should screen across the spectrum, not just for the presenting concern.

### Autism prevalence + epidemiology

Gillberg's group has conducted some of the longest-running autism prevalence studies. His 2010s work documented:

- Rising prevalence (from <0.1% in 1980s screening to ~1-2% by 2010s) — interpreted as primarily diagnostic-recognition shift, with possibly small true-incidence component
- Comparable rates in Sweden and Faroe Islands when consistent diagnostic criteria applied
- High comorbidity with ADHD, ID, and motor coordination disorder

### Asperger syndrome description

Gillberg was instrumental in **operationalizing diagnostic criteria for Asperger syndrome** in the late 1980s — work that brought recognition of the higher-functioning end of the autism spectrum into clinical practice. The DSM-5 absorbed Asperger into autism spectrum disorder (2013), but Gillberg's earlier criteria remain clinically influential in Europe.

### Female autism + lifespan recognition

Gillberg has been an early voice on under-recognition of autism in females — contributing to today's understanding that the male:female autism ratio is closer to 2:1 or 3:1 than the historically-cited 4:1 once diagnostic criteria are applied without sex bias.

## Notes

The Gillberg Neuropsychiatry Centre in Gothenburg is a major training site for European child psychiatrists working in autism. Gillberg's clinical orientation toward integrated recognition — rather than narrow DSM categorization — aligns with the atlas's phenotype-centric framework.
