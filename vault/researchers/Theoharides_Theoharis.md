# Theoharis Theoharides, MD, PhD — Mast cells, neuroinflammation, and luteolin

Theoharis Theoharides has spent decades on the mast cell as a fundamental, under-appreciated regulator of neuroinflammation and brain function. His autism contribution is the documentation that **mast cell activation — beyond classical histamine/tryptase mediator release — drives a substantial portion of the neuroinflammation and dysautonomia seen in autism**, and that mast cells and microglia function as a coupled "master conductor" of brain inflammation.

## Why he matters

Mast cells are best known to clinicians as the histamine-release cells in allergy. Theoharides's work establishes them as much more: they release more than 200 distinct mediators (histamine, tryptase, chymase, cytokines including IL-6 and TNF-α, neurotrophins, neuropeptides, ATP, and more), they are densely populated in the brain (particularly the hypothalamus and meninges), and they cross-talk with microglia to amplify neuroinflammatory signaling.

In autism specifically:

- A documented subgroup of autistic children meets criteria for mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS), with elevated tryptase, methylhistamine, prostaglandin D2, chromogranin A.
- Mast cell activation correlates with autonomic dysfunction (heart rate variability changes, GI dysmotility, sleep disturbance), with allergic / atopic comorbidity (eczema, food allergies, asthma), and with neurobehavioral severity.
- Perinatal stress and maternal infection activate fetal mast cells.
- Microglial activation in the autistic brain — documented in postmortem studies and PET imaging — couples directly to mast-cell-derived signals.

## Key recent work

**2023–2025 reviews and primary papers** — Multiple pieces extending the mast-cell-microglia coupling story into autism, dysautonomia, and (via the 2020–2025 long-COVID literature) post-viral neuroinflammatory syndromes that share mechanism with regressive autism.

**2024–2025 brain tissue analyses in ASD** — Mentioned in conference talks; quantification of mast cell density / activation markers in postmortem autistic brain tissue.

**Luteolin formulation work** — Theoharides has spent years developing and clinically testing flavonoid-based mast-cell stabilizers, particularly luteolin (a polyphenolic flavone that crosses the blood-brain barrier and stabilizes both mast cells and microglia in vitro and in animal models). Multiple proprietary luteolin-based formulations exist (NeuroProtek and similar). Open-label clinical experience in autism with mast-cell activation features has been favorable; randomized trials are limited.

## Core concepts

**Master conductor of neuroinflammation.** Mast cells and microglia, working together, set the brain's inflammatory tone. They sit at the intersection of the immune system, the autonomic nervous system, and the blood-brain barrier.

**Perinatal stress / infection as triggers.** Maternal infection during pregnancy, perinatal stress, and early-life infections all activate mast cells. This is one of the mechanistic substrates underlying [[HYP-0008 Maternal immune activation (prenatal infection or autoimmune)]].

**Long-COVID overlap.** The post-acute syndrome from COVID infection shares mechanism with regressive autism through this same mast-cell / microglial axis. This has expanded the audience for mast-cell stabilization protocols.

**Flavonoids as mast cell stabilizers.** Luteolin specifically — but also quercetin, fisetin, rutin — stabilize mast cells and microglia in vitro. The bioavailability problem has been the main translational obstacle; Theoharides's formulation work has addressed this for luteolin specifically.

## Practical takeaway for parents

If your child has any of: chronic allergic / atopic disease (eczema, asthma, food allergies, frequent rashes), GI dysmotility, sleep disturbance, dysautonomia (heart rate / blood pressure / temperature regulation issues), flushing, abdominal pain, or any otherwise-unexplained inflammatory presentation alongside autism — get an MCAS workup. Tryptase (baseline + acute), urinary methylhistamine, prostaglandin D2, chromogranin A. A knowledgeable allergist/immunologist or MCAS-experienced physician is the right consult.

If MCAS is established, mast cell stabilization protocol typically includes:

- **H1 antihistamines** (cetirizine, fexofenadine, hydroxyzine) — daily.
- **H2 antihistamines** (famotidine).
- **Cromolyn sodium** — pre-meal, oral. Stabilizes mast cells in the GI tract.
- **Ketotifen** — combined H1 / mast-cell-stabilizing antihistamine.
- **Luteolin** (Theoharides-type formulation) — emerging therapy specifically for the brain-side mast-cell story.
- **Quercetin** — adjunct.
- **Low-histamine diet** — under nutritionist supervision.
- **Avoidance of mast-cell triggers** — alcohol, certain medications, environmental triggers identified per individual.

This is one of the more under-recognized and treatable autism subgroup phenotypes. The Theoharides framework + a knowledgeable clinician + standard MCAS workup can produce substantial quality-of-life improvement in the right children.
