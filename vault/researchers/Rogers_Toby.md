# Toby Rogers, PhD — Political-economy of the autism epidemic

Toby Rogers is a political economist (PhD, UC Berkeley) who has built one of the most rigorous public-economics frameworks for understanding **why the autism epidemic exists at the institutional level** — not the biological level. His core argument: autism is now affecting somewhere between 1 in 30 and 1 in 12 children depending on which jurisdiction and which subset; this is unprecedented and is the result of regulatory failure, not natural variation; and the institutional incentives that produced it are still operating.

## Why he matters

Rogers is one of the few researchers framing the autism question at the right scale. The biological mechanisms ([[Naviaux_Robert]], [[Frye_Richard]], [[Hazan_Sabine]] and others) explain *how* a vulnerable child develops autism. Rogers's framework explains *why so many vulnerable children are being created in the first place*, and what would have to change at the policy level to reverse it.

## Key work

**Substack (ongoing)** — Rogers writes long-form pieces analyzing autism prevalence trends, public-health policy failures, and proposed reforms. One of the most careful political-economy treatments of public health regulatory capture.

**Cost-of-autism economic modeling (2023, retracted)** — Rogers published a peer-reviewed economic analysis estimating the cost of the autism epidemic. The paper was retracted on conflict-of-interest / methodological-disclosure grounds, with Rogers maintaining that the underlying calculations are correct and the retraction was procedural rather than substantive.

**323-fold autism risk increase since the 1950s** — Rogers's headline statistic, derived from comparing pre-1980s autism prevalence (when it was roughly 1 in 10,000) to current prevalence (now under 1 in 36 nationwide and substantially higher in some subgroups). Rogers argues this magnitude of increase cannot be explained by diagnostic expansion alone.

**2025 Senate testimony** — On the autism epidemic, regulatory capture, and reform recommendations.

**The "Rogers Test"** — A heuristic Rogers proposes for evaluating credible causation claims in autism: the candidate cause must (1) have temporal correlation with the rise in prevalence, (2) have plausible biological mechanism, (3) have sufficient population exposure to account for the case load, and (4) have an institutional explanation for why it would be under-studied or denied.

## Concept summary

**Regulatory capture.** Public-health institutions (FDA, CDC, ACIP) operate under industry capture: revolving-door personnel, industry-funded studies, liability shields (the 1986 Vaccine Injury Act), and conflicting incentive structures. The result is that exposures that benefit industry get inadequate safety study and the studies that do get done are systematically biased.

**Whole-society toxicant reduction.** Rogers's prevention frame is broad: reduce environmental load on developing children. Vaccine schedule reform is part of this but is not the entire frame. Glyphosate, food-system industrial chemicals, environmental endocrine disruptors, and prenatal care interventions are all part of the same picture.

**Autism as canary, not endpoint.** Rogers frames autism as the visible symptom of a larger developmental-health crisis. ADHD, allergies, autoimmune conditions, infertility, and adolescent mental health are all in the same constellation. Institutional reform that addresses the autism driver should help the rest.
