# Robert Naviaux, MD, PhD — The Cell Danger Response and the 3-Hit framework

Robert Naviaux runs the UCSD Mitochondrial and Metabolic Disease Center and is the architect of the **Cell Danger Response (CDR)** theory — arguably the most ambitious and unifying mechanistic framework in autism research today. His 2026 update, the **3-Hit Metabolic Signaling Model**, has been ingested into the atlas as [[SRC-001448 41902612]] and now anchors four hypothesis families simultaneously (mitochondrial dysfunction, maternal immune activation, polygenic risk, developmental timing).

## Why he matters

Naviaux is the closest thing autism research has to a unifying field theory. His framework explains, in a single mechanistic structure, why:

- Genetic vulnerability matters but isn't deterministic.
- Environmental triggers (infection, immune activation, toxicants) matter but only within a critical window.
- Effect heterogeneity is the rule, not the exception.
- Persistent "stuck" metabolic states can produce reversible developmental trajectories.

The framework is biochemistry-grounded. The CDR is initiated by extracellular ATP (eATP) and purinergic signaling (P2X / P2Y receptors), and is normally a finite, self-limiting cellular response to threat. When it doesn't shut off — because the threat is persistent, recurrent, or because the cell's mitochondrial / Ca²⁺ / purinergic machinery is genetically or epigenetically sensitized — the cell stays in a defensive metabolic state. In a developing brain during a critical window, this prevents normal neurodevelopmental state transitions from occurring.

Hence: *autism as a developmental state-transition disorder driven by stuck CDR.*

## The 3-Hit framework (2026 update)

Per [[SRC-001448 41902612]] (Naviaux 2026, *Autism Research*, peer-reviewed), autism arises from sequential interaction of three hits:

**Hit 1 — Inherited genetic / epigenetic sensitization.** Variants in mitochondrial, calcium-handling, or purinergic-signaling genes that lower the threshold for entering the CDR state and raise the threshold for exiting it. This includes both rare high-impact variants (e.g., the [[SFARI Tier 1 + 2]] gene set) and common polygenic risk ([[HYP-0028 Inherited polygenic risk]]). Maternal mitochondrial inheritance is heavily implicated.

**Hit 2 — Early CDR activation.** A prenatal or early-postnatal trigger that activates the CDR. Naviaux is explicit that "trigger" is broad: maternal infection, maternal immune activation ([[HYP-0008 Maternal immune activation (prenatal infection or autoimmune)]]), metabolic disturbance, toxicant exposure, anything that starts the cell-danger response.

**Hit 3 — Persistent or recurrent CDR triggers during the critical window.** Specifically: 3–6 months of CDR activation occurring sometime between the late first trimester and 18–36 months of age. The window matters because that's when the relevant developmental state transitions are happening. Persistent CDR during the window prevents those transitions from occurring normally.

## PKU as proof-of-principle

Naviaux specifically cites phenylketonuria (PKU) as proof that this kind of system is interruptible. Untreated PKU historically caused intellectual disability and autistic features. Universal newborn screening + early dietary phenylalanine restriction interrupts the metabolic insult during the critical window, and prevents/decreases the developmental phenotype.

The 3-Hit framework predicts that the same kind of interruption — at the level of CDR activation, in the right window — should be possible for autism.

## Key prior work

**Naviaux 2014 (PMID 23981537)** — *"Metabolic features of the cell danger response."* Mitochondrion. The foundational CDR paper. Establishes the biochemistry of eATP / purinergic signaling / mitochondrial response to threat.

**Naviaux 2017 suramin trial (PMID 28695149)** — *"Low-dose suramin in autism spectrum disorder: a small, phase I/II, randomized clinical trial."* Annals of Clinical and Translational Neurology. Small RCT of low-dose suramin (an antipurinergic agent) in autistic children. Statistically significant improvements in core autism symptoms. Limited by toxicity profile and lack of follow-up large-scale trial. Concept-validating but not yet a deployable intervention.

**Naviaux 2026 (PMID 41902612)** — The 3-Hit framework synthesis. Already in atlas as [[SRC-001448 41902612]].

## Practical takeaway for parents

The framework is more theoretical than directly actionable today, but it has clear practical implications:

1. **Reduce environmental CDR triggers during the critical window** (late first trimester through 18–36 months): minimize unnecessary infections, address maternal immune activation, reduce toxicant exposure, support mitochondrial function. This is the *prevention* arm.
2. **Support mitochondrial function** in mito-vulnerable kids: Rossignol's mito cocktail, dietary support, avoidance of mitotoxic medications. See [[Rossignol_Daniel]].
3. **CDR-shifting interventions** are an emerging but not yet deployable category. Suramin showed proof-of-concept but isn't widely available. Future antipurinergic agents are in early development.
4. **The framework reframes how to think about treatment.** If autism is partially a stuck metabolic state in a critical window, then interventions that reduce ongoing CDR activation (reduce inflammation, reduce oxidative stress, reduce environmental load on mitochondria) should be additively beneficial — and that's exactly what the field's most successful subgroup interventions ([[Frye_Richard]] leucovorin, [[Hazan_Sabine]] microbiome, [[Theoharides_Theoharis]] mast cell stabilization) all do.

Naviaux's framework is the connective tissue across the entire functional-medicine autism research community.
