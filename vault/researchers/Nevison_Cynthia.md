# Cynthia Nevison, PhD — Quantitative analysis of the autism prevalence rise

Cynthia Nevison is an atmospheric scientist (University of Colorado Boulder) who, applying quantitative trend-analysis methodology from her primary field, has produced one of the most rigorous published analyses of the **autism prevalence rise from the 1980s through the present**, and the relative contributions of diagnostic-expansion versus genuine incidence increase.

## Why she matters

The mainstream public-health framing is that the rise in autism prevalence is *primarily* an artifact of diagnostic expansion (broader criteria, better identification, greater awareness). Nevison's analysis contradicts this: the rise cannot be explained by diagnostic factors alone, and a substantial component represents real incidence increase requiring environmental explanation. This finding is foundational to the [[Rogers_Toby]] political-economy framework and to the broader contested-evidence cluster.

## Key work

**Nevison 2014** — *"A comparison of temporal trends in United States autism prevalence to trends in suspected environmental factors."* Comprehensive trend analysis comparing autism prevalence trajectory against trajectories of suspected environmental contributors (mercury exposure, glyphosate, vaccine schedule additions, electronic media, etc.). Methodologically rigorous; quantitatively careful.

**Multiple subsequent papers** — Refining the trend analysis, addressing the diagnostic-expansion question quantitatively, and characterizing real vs apparent prevalence change.

**Environmental-contribution modeling** — Statistical approaches to disentangling environmental from genetic contributions to the prevalence rise.

## Concept summary

**Autism prevalence trajectory.** Pre-1980s autism prevalence was approximately 1 in 10,000. Current US prevalence is approximately 1 in 36 nationwide (CDC 2023 ADDM Network) and substantially higher in some California counties (estimates approaching 1 in 20 boys in some districts). The rise is roughly 100–300-fold over four decades.

**Diagnostic expansion vs real increase.** Some of the rise is diagnostic expansion (broader DSM criteria, particularly DSM-IV → DSM-5, and increased identification of higher-functioning cases). But Nevison's analysis demonstrates that diagnostic factors cannot explain the magnitude of the rise. A substantial component is real incidence increase.

**Implication.** If real incidence is rising, environmental contribution must be substantial — genetics doesn't change at population scale on this timeline. This is the quantitative foundation for the Tier 2 contested-evidence cluster's broader argument.
