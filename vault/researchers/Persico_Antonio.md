# Prof. Antonio Persico

**Affiliation:** Professor + Director, Child & Adolescent Neuropsychiatry, University of Modena and Reggio Emilia, Italy

## Why this researcher matters

Persico established **urinary p-cresol as a biomarker for autism severity** — the canonical reference for the gut-microbial-metabolite hypothesis of autism. His 2010 finding that p-cresol is elevated in young autistic children, with severity correlation, opened the entire microbiome-metabolite-autism research field. p-cresol is now well-characterized as a [[BIO-0038|primary atlas biomarker]] for the [[PHE-0004 Autism + GI _ microbiome phenotype|gut-microbiome phenotype]].

## Key contributions

### Urinary p-cresol as autism biomarker

Persico's 2010 paper ([PMID 21329489](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21329489/)) reported that urinary p-cresol (4-cresol) is significantly elevated in children with autism — particularly:

- **Younger autistic children** (<8 years)
- **Females more than males** when controlling for severity
- **More severely affected** regardless of sex

This was foundational because:
- p-cresol cannot be produced by human metabolism — it is **exclusively microbial**
- Specifically, **Clostridia** species (especially C. difficile) produce p-cresol
- Elevated urinary p-cresol therefore implicates **microbial dysbiosis** in autism severity
- The biomarker is non-invasive (urine) and quantifiable

### p-cresol mechanism work

Subsequent mechanistic work, partly from Persico's lab and collaborators, established that p-cresol:
- **Inhibits dopamine β-hydroxylase** → relative norepinephrine deficit
- **Disrupts dopamine metabolism** in striatum
- **Crosses BBB** — directly affects CNS function
- **Induces autism-like behaviors** in mouse models when administered chronically (multiple groups have replicated this)

### Mouse model translation

The propionic acid + p-cresol mouse model framework — pioneered in part by Persico's collaborators with [[MacFabe_Derrick|MacFabe]] — provides the preclinical justification for treating microbial metabolites as a *causal* contributor to autism phenotype, not just a correlate.

## Notes

Persico's lab has been an Italian autism research anchor for two decades, with a clinical service at the University Campus Bio-Medico in Rome (earlier in his career) and now at Modena. The Italian autism research community is small but high-impact — Persico's p-cresol work and his clinical genetics studies (including work on serotonin transporter and BDNF polymorphisms) have shaped European autism biology research.
