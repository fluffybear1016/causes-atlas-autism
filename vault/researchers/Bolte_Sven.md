# Prof. Sven Bölte

**Affiliation:** Professor, Karolinska Institutet — Center of Neurodevelopmental Disorders (KIND); founder + Director, Roots of Autism and ADHD Twin Study in Sweden (RATSS)

## Why this researcher matters

Bölte runs the **RATSS twin cohort** — one of the most powerful autism + ADHD twin samples in the world (especially monozygotic discordant + concordant pairs). The RATSS design directly tests genetic-vs-environmental contributions to autism + ADHD at the individual phenotype level. He is also a leading voice on **female-presentation autism** and the high rate of misdiagnosis / under-diagnosis in girls and women.

## Key contributions

### RATSS — Roots of Autism and ADHD Twin Study in Sweden

Bölte built RATSS as a deep-phenotyping twin cohort:

- **Both monozygotic and dizygotic** twin pairs
- **Both concordant and discordant** for autism / ADHD
- Phenotyping spans: psychometrics, MRI, genetics, biomarkers, environmental exposure history
- Designed to identify environmental factors that explain phenotype divergence in genetically-identical twins

This is methodologically important because it can identify **modifiable environmental contributors** — exactly the data the [[Hannah Poling framework]] needs.

### Autism × ADHD co-occurrence

Bölte's work has been influential in establishing that autism + ADHD co-occur far more than chance — and that this co-occurrence has shared genetic + environmental architecture. The DSM-5 finally permitted dual ASD + ADHD diagnosis (2013) in part because of this evidence base.

### Female-presentation autism

Bölte has been a vocal proponent of recognizing the **female autism phenotype** — masking, internalized presentation, late diagnosis, and ADHD-mimicking features. His work has contributed to clinical recognition that the male:female ratio in autism (historically 4:1) likely overestimates true sex difference because of diagnostic bias.

### Neurodevelopmental disorder transdiagnostic frameworks

Bölte has advanced the transdiagnostic perspective: autism, ADHD, intellectual disability, motor coordination disorder, and language disorder share substantial genetic + environmental architecture and are best understood on a continuum of neurodevelopmental conditions, not as discrete categories.
