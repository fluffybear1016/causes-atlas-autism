# James Adams, PhD — Microbiota Transfer Therapy and the long-term durability question

James Adams runs the autism research program at Arizona State University and is the lead investigator on the **Microbiota Transfer Therapy (MTT)** trials — the most formal, peer-reviewed clinical-trial program for microbiome intervention in autism. While [[Hazan_Sabine]] is doing precision sibling-donor familial FMT under FDA IND, Adams has run the structured RCT-style MTT program that produced the foundational peer-reviewed literature on which the broader microbiome-autism field stands.

## Why he matters

The MTT program is the rigorous, formally-published evidence backbone for "microbiome intervention works in autism." Without Adams's published trials, the entire microbiome-ASD intervention field would not have institutional credibility. With them, the question is no longer *whether* microbiome intervention can help autistic children, but *which subgroup, what protocol, what donor source, how long.*

## Key work

**Kang et al. 2017 (PMID 28122648)** — *"Microbiota Transfer Therapy alters gut ecosystem and improves gastrointestinal and autism symptoms: an open-label study."* 18 children with autism + GI symptoms. Two-week MTT protocol (oral and rectal microbiome preparations from a healthy human donor pool, structured antibiotic preconditioning, prolonged maintenance dosing). **80% reduction in GI symptoms and significant behavioral improvements at 8-week follow-up.**

**Kang et al. 2019 (PMID 30967657)** — *"Long-term benefit of Microbiota Transfer Therapy on autism symptoms and gut microbiota."* Scientific Reports. 2-year follow-up of the same cohort. **GI improvements largely sustained. Autism behavioral scores continued to improve over the 2-year window.** This is the durability paper. It's the key piece of evidence that microbiome intervention is not a transient bump.

**2025 metabolite reanalysis** (with Krajmalnik-Brown and collaborators) — Reanalysis of the MTT cohort showing that MTT modulates specific fecal and plasma metabolites that correlate with the behavioral changes. This bridges the "microbiome composition changed" → "metabolites changed" → "brain function changed" causal chain.

**Phase 2 trials ongoing** — Both pediatric and adult MTT trials currently enrolling / in analysis as of 2025–2026. Larger N, more standardized outcome measures.

## Concept summary

**MTT is structured, not freeform.** Unlike Hazan's case-by-case sibling-donor approach, the Kang/Adams MTT protocol uses: pooled healthy donor preparations, a specific 2-week antibiotic preconditioning + microbiota dosing schedule, and a prolonged oral maintenance phase. It's designed to be replicable in a clinical-trial setting.

**Outcome biomarkers include both microbiome composition and downstream metabolites.** This is what the 2025 reanalysis nailed down: not just "the microbiome looked different," but specific bile-acid, SCFA, and tryptophan-pathway metabolites changed in directions that correlate with behavioral improvement.

**Long-term durability is a real finding.** The 2-year follow-up is among the most important findings in autism intervention research. It refutes the lazy critique that any improvement after FMT/MTT is just placebo or transient.

## Practical takeaway for parents

If you want a peer-reviewed-trial-grounded entry point into microbiome intervention, the MTT line at Arizona State is it. Phase 2 trials are open. Selection criteria, structured protocol, formal outcome measures.

If your child fits the trial criteria, this is the most rigorous pathway available right now. If not, the next most relevant options are Hazan's IND-protocol familial FMT and a discussion with a knowledgeable functional-medicine GI specialist about commercial FMT products (which are typically only approved for C. diff, but precedent exists for case-by-case off-label use under careful supervision).
