# Stephanie Seneff, PhD — Glyphosate, PIN1, and the sulfur-pathway etiology

Stephanie Seneff is a senior research scientist at MIT (computer science by training; biological systems modeling by extensive autodidactic redirection). Her autism contribution is a sustained mechanistic argument that **glyphosate — the active ingredient in Roundup and the most heavily-used agrochemical in the world — is a major upstream driver of autism via disruption of sulfur metabolism, the gut microbiome, methylation pathways, and (most recently) the PIN1 prolyl isomerase enzyme critical for synaptic structure.**

## Why she matters

Glyphosate is on every supermarket shelf in the form of glyphosate residues in conventional grain, soy, corn, and oats. Cumulative pediatric exposure from food and environment is substantial. Seneff's mechanistic argument — that glyphosate disrupts multiple pathways that other autism researchers (Frye on methylation, Hazan on microbiome, Rossignol on mitochondrial function) have independently identified as compromised in autism — is one of the more parsimonious upstream-driver hypotheses available.

She is one of the very few researchers continuing to publish primary mechanistic work on glyphosate-autism etiology in peer-reviewed journals despite the industry pushback this work has generated.

## Key work

**Seneff 2024 (J Neurochem)** — *"Is autism a PIN1 deficiency syndrome? A proposed etiological role for glyphosate."* Mechanistic argument: glyphosate disrupts PIN1 (peptidyl-prolyl isomerase NIMA-interacting 1), which is critical for dendritic spine density and morphology. Reduced PIN1 activity is consistent with the dendritic-spine and synaptic-pruning anomalies documented in autistic brain tissue. This is her most novel mechanistic contribution.

**Toxic Legacy (2022 book)** — Comprehensive synthesis of the glyphosate-disease case. Builds on prior peer-reviewed work on glyphosate and Western diseases.

**Earlier and broader work (2010s onward)** — Multiple peer-reviewed pieces on glyphosate's effects on cytochrome P450 enzymes (which detoxify many xenobiotics), gut microbiome shikimate-pathway disruption, sulfate metabolism, and methylation cycle integrity. Her general thesis: glyphosate is much more biologically active in mammals than its regulatory approval acknowledged because the shikimate pathway it targets is also operative in gut bacteria, and gut bacteria provide critical biosynthetic functions for the human host.

**Continuing primary research output (2023–2026)** — Publishes despite controversy. The 2024 PIN1 paper is a representative example of work that is mechanistically dense, computationally argued, and frequently dismissed by mainstream toxicology while being increasingly difficult to refute on the underlying biochemistry.

## Concept summary

**Glyphosate disrupts multiple autism-relevant pathways.**

1. *Sulfate transport.* Glyphosate's structure mimics glycine; it is hypothesized to substitute for glycine in proteins and disrupt sulfate transport, which is critical for brain extracellular matrix and detoxification.
2. *Gut microbiome shikimate pathway.* Glyphosate's primary mechanism (in plants and some bacteria) is shikimate-pathway inhibition. This pathway is operative in many human gut bacteria, including Bifidobacterium species ([[Hazan_Sabine]]'s key target). Glyphosate exposure systematically depletes Bifidobacterium and shifts the microbiome toward dysbiotic patterns.
3. *Methylation cycle.* Glyphosate disrupts methionine and S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) production, which compromises methylation and intersects with the [[Frye_Richard]] / [[Walsh_William]] frameworks.
4. *Cytochrome P450 inhibition.* Reduces detoxification capacity for environmental chemicals and pharmaceuticals.
5. *PIN1 disruption (2024 paper).* Affects synaptic structure and dendritic spine density.

**Cumulative pediatric exposure.** Glyphosate residues are ubiquitous in conventional grain-based foods. Glyphosate is also used as a preharvest desiccant on wheat and oats, dramatically increasing residues in the final product. Children consuming standard American diets are exposed daily.

**Temporal correlation with autism prevalence.** The dramatic rise in glyphosate use since the introduction of Roundup-Ready GMO crops in the mid-1990s temporally aligns with the rise in autism prevalence over the same period. Seneff treats this as one piece of evidence among several rather than the smoking gun.

## Practical takeaway for parents

Glyphosate exposure is one of the most actionable environmental modifiers available:

- **Buy organic for the worst offenders:** wheat, oats (especially conventional oats — heavily desiccated with glyphosate before harvest), corn, soy, sugar (sugar beets are GMO Roundup-Ready). Organic certification prohibits glyphosate.
- **Rotate animal proteins and dairy toward grass-fed / pasture-raised** where possible — conventional feedlot animals are fed glyphosate-residue grain.
- **Glyphosate testing** is available (HRI Labs, Great Plains Laboratory, others) — urinary glyphosate measurement gives a snapshot of recent exposure.
- **Glyphosate-detoxification supports** are case-by-case but include: glycine supplementation (literally outcompetes glyphosate for some binding sites), sulfate/molybdenum/B12 cofactor support, microbiome restoration, fiber intake.

This is one of the lowest-hanging environmental modifiers available to families. Effect sizes are subgroup-dependent but the floor is low (organic food doesn't hurt anything) and the ceiling is potentially substantial.
