# Prof. Sven Sandin

**Affiliation:** Associate Professor, Karolinska Institutet (Sweden); Mount Sinai (USA); leader of the Autism and Perinatal Epidemiology Research Group

## Why this researcher matters

Sandin runs the largest perinatal autism epidemiology program in the world, leveraging Sweden's complete population registries (every child born in Sweden, with full health and pregnancy records). His **heritability estimate of ~50%** for autism (with the remaining 50% from non-shared environmental factors) is the field-defining number, derived from a 2 million-child Swedish national cohort.

This work is **uniquely powerful for the [[Hannah Poling framework]]** — Swedish registry data can detect modifiable perinatal exposures (medication, infection, nutrition, maternal disease) that condition autism risk in genetically-susceptible subsets.

## Key contributions

### Heritability estimate (50%)

The Sandin / Reichenberg / Hultman paper in *JAMA* (2017) analyzed all 2 million children born in Sweden 1982-2006 (14,516 with autism diagnoses) and estimated:

- **Autism heritability: ~50%**
- **Non-shared environmental factors: ~50%**
- **Shared family environment: small to negligible**

The interpretation: half of autism risk comes from genetics, but the *other* half is environmental — and importantly, **non-shared environmental factors** dominate. This means twins and siblings share genetics + family environment but can have different outcomes because of different individual exposures (in utero, perinatal, postnatal).

This is the empirical foundation for the modifiable-exposure framework that drives most of the atlas's intervention layer.

### Perinatal risk factor analysis

Sandin's group has analyzed Swedish registry data for autism risk associated with:

- **Maternal infection / hospitalization during pregnancy** — converges with [[Atladottir 2010]] findings
- **Maternal autoimmune disease** — confirmed risk increment
- **Maternal medication exposure** (SSRIs, valproate, acetaminophen) — varied effects
- **Paternal age** — consistent risk increment (mosaic mutations + sperm DNA damage hypothesis)
- **Birth complications** — preterm, low birth weight, perinatal hypoxia
- **Cesarean section** — modest signal in some analyses

### Sibling recurrence risk

Sandin's group has refined sibling recurrence estimates: ~20% recurrence for younger siblings of an autism child (vs ~1.5% population baseline) — the basis for genetic counseling estimates in autism families.
