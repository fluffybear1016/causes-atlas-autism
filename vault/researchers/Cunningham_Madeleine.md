# Madeleine Cunningham, PhD — The Cunningham Panel and PANS/PANDAS biomarker

Madeleine Cunningham is the immunologist who developed the **Cunningham Panel** — the anti-neuronal antibody test that operationalizes [[Swedo_Sue]]'s PANS / PANDAS diagnostic framework. Her work demonstrates that a subgroup of children with sudden-onset neuropsychiatric symptoms (including some labeled as "regressive autism") have measurable antibodies cross-reacting with brain neuronal antigens, and that these antibodies predict response to immune-targeted therapy.

## Why she matters

Cunningham's work converts PANS/PANDAS from a clinical syndrome diagnosis into a **biomarker-stratified diagnosis with a treatable mechanism**. Without her panel, the syndrome would still be a clinical-only diagnosis (which mainstream pediatric medicine has historically been skeptical of). With it, there is a measurable lab marker that predicts which children will respond to immune therapy, putting it on a footing similar to what [[Frye_Richard]]'s FRAA work did for cerebral folate deficiency.

## Key work

**Anti-neuronal antibody discovery (1990s–2000s)** — Cunningham's lab demonstrated that antibodies generated against *Streptococcus* cross-react with basal ganglia neuronal antigens (specifically tubulin, lysoganglioside, dopamine D1 receptor, dopamine D2 receptor, and CaMKII activation). This is the molecular-mimicry mechanism that explains how a streptococcal infection can trigger a chronic neuropsychiatric syndrome.

**The Cunningham Panel** — Commercial clinical lab test (Moleculera Biosciences) measuring anti-tubulin, anti-lysoganglioside, anti-dopamine D1, anti-dopamine D2, and CaMKII activation antibodies. The panel produces both a quantitative antibody profile and a "CaMKII activation score" that correlates with disease activity. Used clinically to support PANS/PANDAS diagnosis and to monitor response to therapy.

**Multiple peer-reviewed publications** — Establishing the cross-reactivity mechanism, characterizing the antibody profile across PANS/PANDAS cases, and demonstrating the panel's diagnostic utility.

**Continuing through 2023–2026** — Active research and clinical-test development.

### Reliability critique (important caveat)

A peer-reviewed critique (Hesselmark and Bejerot, *Translational Psychiatry* 2019) reported elevated CaMKII activity in 48% of healthy controls and at least one positive antibody titer in many controls, raising questions about the panel's specificity at the cutoffs used commercially. The Cunningham group and others have responded with their own analyses defending the panel's clinical utility when interpreted alongside symptom course.

The atlas's position: the Cunningham Panel is the most widely-deployed PANS/PANDAS biomarker test, the underlying biology (anti-neuronal antibody cross-reactivity) is well-established, and clinically-positive results in symptomatic children predict response to immune therapy. But: positive results in asymptomatic individuals should not be over-interpreted, and the panel works best as a *confirmatory* test in clinically-suspected cases rather than a screening test in low-prior-probability populations. Per [[CLAUDE]] §1, both the panel's clinical utility and the published reliability concerns are recorded.

## Concept summary

**Molecular mimicry.** The five Cunningham Panel antigens are neuronal proteins that share epitope similarity with bacterial (and viral) surface proteins. Antibodies generated against the infection cross-react with the brain. This is the same mechanism that drives rheumatic heart disease (where strep antibodies attack cardiac valve antigens) — Cunningham's broader research career has covered both.

**Antibodies → behavior.** The five-antibody profile correlates with specific symptom clusters. Anti-dopamine antibodies particularly correlate with OCD, tic, and movement-disorder presentations. CaMKII activation correlates with overall disease severity.

**Treatment response prediction.** Children with elevated Cunningham Panel scores respond more reliably to immune therapy (NSAIDs, antibiotics, IVIG, plasmapheresis, LDN) than children without elevated antibodies — exactly what biomarker-stratified intervention predicts.

## Practical takeaway for parents

If your child has sudden-onset OCD, tics, anxiety, eating restriction, sensory regression, or behavioral regression — particularly following an infection — the Cunningham Panel is the biomarker test to ask for. It is available through **Moleculera Biosciences** (moleculera.com) by physician order. Cost is significant (~$1,000+ self-pay) but it is the single most informative test for the PANS/PANDAS workup.

A positive Cunningham Panel directs the clinical workup toward immune-targeted intervention rather than (or in addition to) behavioral or psychiatric medication management.
