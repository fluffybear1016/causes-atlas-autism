# Prof. Abraham Reichenberg

**Affiliation:** Professor of Psychiatry, Environmental Medicine, and Public Health, Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai (NYC); affiliated, Karolinska Institutet

## Why this researcher matters

Reichenberg is the principal collaborator on the **Karolinska + Mount Sinai autism heritability program** — partnering with [[Sandin_Sven|Sven Sandin]] on the foundational Swedish-cohort heritability estimates that anchor field understanding of autism's genetic + environmental architecture. He pioneered the **paternal age effect** in psychiatric disorders (including autism), demonstrating that autism risk increases with paternal age — a finding now consistently replicated and incorporated into genetic counseling.

## Key contributions

### Paternal age effect (2006–present)

Reichenberg's 2006 paper in *Archives of General Psychiatry* documented that **autism risk increases substantially with paternal age**, with:
- ~6× higher autism risk for fathers >40 vs <30 (in original cohort)
- The effect is consistent across multiple replicating cohorts
- Mechanistic interpretation: **mosaic mutations + sperm DNA damage** accumulate with paternal age — generating de novo mutations in offspring

This work directly motivated the genetic field's focus on **de novo mutations** as a major autism risk pathway.

### Heritability + sibling recurrence

Co-author with [[Sandin_Sven|Sandin]] on:
- Heritability estimate ~50% (with non-shared environment ~50%)
- Sibling recurrence ~20%
- Maternal age effect (smaller than paternal but real)
- Twin discordance environmental modifiers

### Maternal autoimmune + infection effects

Reichenberg's group has analyzed Swedish + Israeli cohorts for:
- Maternal autoimmune disease + autism risk
- Maternal hospitalization for infection during pregnancy + autism risk
- Gestational diabetes + autism risk

Each is consistent with the [[Hannah Poling framework|MIA / immune-vulnerability framework]].

### Prenatal environmental exposures

Reichenberg's group has investigated the contribution of:
- Air pollution + autism risk (PM2.5 in particular)
- Maternal vitamin D status
- Maternal medication exposure (particularly SSRIs, valproate)

## Notes

Reichenberg holds the **Bernice and Joseph Tanenbaum Chair in Translational Epidemiology** at Mount Sinai. His Karolinska + Mount Sinai dual affiliation is methodologically valuable: Israeli + Swedish + US data are the three richest autism-relevant epidemiological datasets in the world, and Reichenberg works across all three.
